# Wound care

Wound management in raptors follows general avian principles. Initial treatment includes cleaning, lavaging, and debridement. A dilute (0.05%) chlorhexidine solution is commonly used for initial wound care, with subsequent flushes performed with sterile saline, as repeated chlorhexidine exposure may impede healing. Before flushing any wound, it is important to determine whether it communicates with a pneumatized bone or air sac, as fluid inadvertently entering the respiratory system could compromise ventilation. Avian wounds tend to be relatively dry and crusty rather than exudative; therefore, colloid-type wound dressings that maintain a moist environment are preferred to promote debridement, granulation tissue formation, and re-epithelialization. Various topical products may be used, including silver sulfadiazine cream and medical-grade honey. Bandage change frequency should be initially daily, transitioning to every 2-4 days as healing progresses.
