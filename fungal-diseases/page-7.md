Aspergillosis ranks among the most frequently diagnosed infectious diseases in captive raptors. Certain species are particularly susceptible when immunocompromised by concurrent illness, stress, or the demands of training, and prophylactic antifungal treatment may be considered during hospitalization or periods of heightened stress. Species at elevated risk include juvenile red-tailed hawks, juvenile golden eagles, all arctic species (e.g., gyrfalcons, snowy owls), and Northern goshawks. Additional predisposing factors include high environmental fungal spore loads (as occur with hay or straw substrates or poor ventilation), concurrent lead toxicosis, and immunosuppression. Birds undergoing training with significant weight reduction are especially vulnerable.



Aspergillosis is a noncontagious fungal disease primarily associated with management deficiencies and immunocompromised states. Clinical signs frequently observed include nonspecific findings (weight loss, decreased appetite), voice change, abnormal respiratory sounds, labored breathing, dyspnea, and exercise intolerance. A presumptive diagnosis can be reached from whole-body radiographs and/or CT imaging, with CT being substantially more sensitive. Definitive diagnosis is best achieved through coelioscopic visualization with collection of suspicious tissue for cytology, histopathology, and culture. Cytologic identification of typical conidiophores and septate hyphae branching at approximately 45 degrees from the foot cell is diagnostic. Culture without corresponding lesions is not diagnostic, as the organism is ubiquitous and saprophytic.



Treatment with antifungal medications is typically required for several months; serial monitoring of hepatic biochemical parameters is advised when azole drugs are used. Voriconazole is currently considered the treatment of choice for aspergillosis in raptors. Combination therapy may be employed, as some antifungal agents exhibit synergistic activity. Gyrfalcons appear more sensitive to the adverse effects of oral itraconazole, and a reduced dose (7-8 mg/kg PO q12h) may be appropriate for this species. Topical application of amphotericin B directly to visible lesions during endoscopy is recommended. Endoscopic debridement of air sac and syringeal granulomas is also possible and may improve antifungal drug penetration. The overall prognosis is guarded in cases of advanced aspergillosis.
