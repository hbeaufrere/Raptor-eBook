Supportive care in raptors follows the same general principles as for other avian species and includes fluid therapy (subcutaneous, intravenous, or intraosseous), assisted feeding, oxygen supplementation, external heat support, and pain management. Intravenous catheterization is relatively straightforward in raptors given their body size and is less painful than intraosseous catheter placement, though the latter may be safer in certain circumstances due to reduced hemorrhage risk upon removal. Force feeding is accomplished by restraining the bird, opening its beak at the commissure, and placing small pieces of cut prey (e.g., mouse, rat, chick) into the oral cavity caudal to the glottis. Tube feeding is easily performed using a red rubber catheter or large-gauge stainless steel gavage needle inserted into the crop (when present) or directly into the proventriculus. Caution is needed when placing arctic species (e.g., snowy owls, gyrfalcons) in incubators, as they can overheat rapidly.



When extrapolating generic avian drug dosages to raptors, clinicians should recognize that interspecific pharmacokinetic differences can be substantial, and dosages derived from psittacine data may not be appropriate. Species-specific dosing data should be used whenever available. Oral medications may be administered within prey food items when the meal does not interfere with drug absorption. Esophagostomy tubes can be placed to facilitate medication administration in anorexic raptors, reducing the need for repeated restraint.



When gavage feeding emaciated raptors, refeeding syndrome is a potential concern in patients with a prolonged history of anorexia. In raptors, insulin appears to be the dominant hormone governing glycemic homeostasis, in contrast to psittacines and granivorous birds in which glucagon plays a relatively greater role. As a result, refeeding syndrome may be a particularly significant complication in cachexic raptors. Monitoring and correcting plasma electrolyte imbalances is therefore advisable in severely debilitated and emaciated patients. Supplementation with thiamine and magnesium may also be warranted in selected cases.
