The principles of hematologic and biochemical analysis in raptors are the same as for other avian species. Blood is typically collected from the right jugular vein, the ulnar (basilic) veins, or the medial metatarsal veins. The ulnar vein is particularly prone to prolonged hemorrhage and hematoma formation after venipuncture; therefore, extended digital pressure should be applied following needle withdrawal from this site.



Accurate interpretation of clinical pathology results depends in part on the availability of species-specific reference intervals. Hemoparasites are commonly identified in raptors, and blood smears should be evaluated for Haemoproteus spp., Plasmodium spp. (avian malaria), Leucocytozoon spp., and microfilariae. Anemia is a common finding in raptor patients. Anemia of chronic disease is probably the most prevalent cause of anemia in raptors, together with hemorrhage from traumatic injuries. Heterophilic or lymphocytic leukocytosis represents the most frequently observed WBC abnormality in raptors and may be associated with trauma, toxicosis, infection, neoplasia, or inflammation.



A variety of imaging modalities may be employed in raptor patients, including radiography, fluoroscopy, ultrasonography, computed tomography (CT), and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). CT imaging is recommended over traditional radiography whenever available and practical. Endoscopy is a routinely employed diagnostic modality in raptor medicine, useful for evaluating a wide range of conditions and for obtaining targeted biopsy samples.
