# Aviary design

Captive raptors, particularly those kept for falconry, are typically housed in mews (small individual enclosures) or larger flight aviaries. The materials used for enclosure substrates, walls, and perches deserve careful consideration, as inappropriate choices can adversely affect the bird's health. Aviary walls can be constructed from wire mesh, plywood, chloroplast, or other flexible materials capable of absorbing impact if a startled bird strikes the enclosure boundary. Rigid materials risk causing wounds to the head or wings during fright responses. Substrates should be soft to minimize the risk of pododermatitis; preferred options include natural soil, pea gravel, and wood products. To prevent predator entry by digging, aviary walls should extend below ground level, and a concrete foundation covered with dirt or pea gravel is advisable. Perch design is another critical element in preventing foot pathology; perches should vary in shape, diameter, height, and surface texture. Appropriate perch examples include rounded perches wrapped in a padded material (e.g., synthetic turf) and flat wooden platforms positioned at different locations within the flight enclosure. Periodic rotation of perch types within individual enclosures is recommended. In hospital settings, floors, perches, and walls should be constructed of materials that are easily cleaned and disinfected, and the environment should be kept as quiet as possible to minimize stress-related trauma.



Falconry birds are conventionally tethered on specialized perches: "block" perches for falcons and "bow" perches for hawks, with numerous design variations for each. Tropical, subtropical, or migratory species (e.g., Harris's hawks, vultures) housed in cold climates should have access to heated perches during winter. Conversely, arctic species (e.g., snowy owls, gyrfalcons) maintained in warm environments benefit from shaded enclosures with air conditioning and/or ice blocks. Species that are particularly vulnerable to mosquito-borne diseases (often arctic species) may benefit from increased air movement (e.g., fans) over perching and nesting areas to deter mosquitoes. Gyrfalcons are notably susceptible to aspergillosis, and monitoring environmental fungal spore loads is advisable. Substrates known to harbor high concentrations of Aspergillus spp. spores (e.g., hay, straw) should be avoided in transport containers and aviaries. Species prone to self-inflicted trauma or that are easily stressed in captivity (e.g., accipiters) should be housed in enclosures with solid walls to prevent injuries caused by flight panic or escape attempts from handlers.



The captive diet should replicate the bird's natural diet as closely as practicable. It is critical to provide whole prey items to prevent secondary nutritional hyperparathyroidism and other nutritional deficiencies. Certain raptor species (e.g., insectivorous species, falcons) have been shown to develop atherosclerosis when fed diets higher in cholesterol than their natural diet in the wild. Prey animals, especially pigeons and quail, should be frozen and subsequently thawed before feeding to reduce the risk of transmitting certain infectious agents, particularly Trichomonas spp. from pigeons. Because herpesviruses, adenoviruses, and paramyxoviruses are not inactivated by freezing, pigeons should be sourced from reliable suppliers or avoided altogether as food items. Ideally, a variety of prey should be offered, including captive-bred mice, muskrats, gerbils, rats, day-old chicks, quail, and fish (where appropriate for the species). Piscivorous species fed an exclusively fish-based diet require thiamine (vitamin B1) supplementation, especially when fish containing thiaminase enzymes (e.g., capelin, smelt, herring, gizzard shad) are used; these enzymes are concentrated primarily in the prey's viscera. Thiamine supplementation at 25-30 mg/kg of fish, typically injected directly into the prey item, is recommended. Supplemented fish should be offered within 1-2 hours of preparation to minimize degradation of this water-soluble vitamin.



Toxicity from pyridoxine (vitamin B6) has been documented in falcons given doses exceeding 5 mg/kg intramuscularly or 25 mg/kg orally, though the potential contribution of the formulation excipient was not assessed. Insectivorous species (e.g., kestrels, small falcons, kites) should ideally be offered calcium-dusted or wild-caught insects. The amount and type of food provided must be adjusted according to season, ambient temperature, metabolic demands, and the bird's activity level. Prey animals killed with lead ammunition (bullets or pellets) must never be fed to raptors because of the risk of lead toxicosis. Metabolic energy requirements for raptors can be estimated using the formula: MER (kcal) = k x 78 x body weight (kg)^0.75. Fresh water should always be available, even if the birds do not appear to drink. Some species (e.g., falcons, goshawks) bathe daily, so an appropriately sized water container should be placed in the enclosure and cleaned regularly.
