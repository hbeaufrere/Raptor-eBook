# Restraint

Restraint may be necessary for physical examinations, diagnostic imaging, and advanced diagnostic or surgical procedures. Manual restraint, chemical sedation, or general anesthesia may be selected depending on the procedure to be performed and the patient's condition. Restraint duration should be minimized to reduce unnecessary stress. During capture and handling, the feet must be controlled at all times to prevent injury to both the handler and the bird. Injuries inflicted by the beak are generally less severe than those caused by the talons, with the exception of turkey vultures. Leather gloves are generally advisable when catching raptors, particularly wild birds. For eagles and other large species, gloves that extend over the forearms are recommended, along with eye protection.



Raptors may be held in either an upright or recumbent position. The legs should be restrained at the level of the intertarsal joint by one or both hands, with the legs extended caudally. For medium-sized raptors, placing a hood over the head is often sufficient to calm the bird during nonpainful procedures. Studies in red-tailed hawks have demonstrated that hooded birds exhibit significantly lower heart and respiratory rates compared to unhooded birds after 15 minutes of restraint. When prolonged restraint is needed, sedation or general anesthesia should be employed to prevent stress-induced hyperthermia. Arctic species and falcons are especially prone to hyperthermia during handling.



For chemical sedation, midazolam at 1-2 mg/kg may be administered intramuscularly or intranasally and can be combined with an opioid such as butorphanol at 1-2 mg/kg IM. Inhalant anesthesia is generally preferred over injectable techniques because it allows rapid titration of anesthetic depth in response to the patient's status. Ideally, raptors should be fasted for a minimum of 12 hours before anesthesia. In non-fasted patients, endotracheal intubation is recommended using a noncuffed tube, given the complete tracheal rings in birds.
