### A complete external physical examination should be conducted efficiently to minimize stress or performed while the patient is sedated or anesthetized. For birds in respiratory distress, coelomic palpation should be performed early to identify potential extra-respiratory causes of dyspnea, such as eggs, other coelomic masses, or peritoneal effusion. Body condition is assessed by palpating the pectoral muscles, as in other birds. Hydration status is evaluated using several parameters, including ulnar vein diameter and refill time, the presence of viscous saliva strands in the oral cavity, and skin turgor over the pectoral region. Cloacal temperature should be measured, especially in species prone to stress-induced hyperthermia (e.g., falcons). Normal cloacal temperature ranges from approximately 39-41 degrees C (102-106 degrees F) in Falconiformes and Accipitriformes and from 38-39 degrees C (100-102 degrees F) in Strigiformes.

###

### The musculoskeletal system should be systematically palpated for fractures, luxations, and muscular asymmetry. The plantar surface of the feet is examined by extending the digits while keeping the leg fully extended to reduce the bird's grip strength. Plumage should be inspected for missing feathers, abnormal coloration, and structural defects. The plantar surface of the feet should be carefully inspected for hyperkeratosis, abrasions, crusts, or flattening of the papillary ridges, all of which may indicate early pododermatitis.
