# Special senses

Ocular diseases and traumatic eye injuries are exceptionally common in these visually dependent avian species. Accordingly, the anatomy and visual physiology of raptors have been the subject of extensive investigation, with numerous reviews available in the literature.



The shape of the ocular globe differs among raptor groups: Falconiformes, Accipitriformes, and Cathartiformes have a globose eye, while in Strigiformes it is tubular. The fundamental eye anatomy is similar to that of other birds and includes a third eyelid (nictitating membrane), an avascular (anangiotic) retina with a pecten, and voluntarily controlled pupillary muscles owing to the presence of striated irideal musculature. Strigiformes differ markedly from other raptors in having a tubular eye that is externally reinforced by scleral ossicles substantially larger than those in other birds. The retina is not pigmented, and choroidal vasculature is readily visible on fundoscopic examination. Owls have a rod-dominant retina (enabling effective crepuscular and nocturnal vision), whereas diurnal raptors have a cone-dominant retina. No raptor species possesses a retinal tapetum lucidum. As in other birds, all raptors have a pecten oculi in the fundus. The irido-corneal angle is readily visualized in owls owing to their deep anterior chamber. The lateral aspect of the ocular globe constitutes the medial wall of the external ear canal in owls. Visual acuity in most raptors greatly exceeds that of typical mammals, and all raptor species possess some degree of binocular vision.
