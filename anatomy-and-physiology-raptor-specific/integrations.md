# General anatomy of birds

The anatomy and physiology of raptors largely parallels that of other avian orders; therefore, only clinically significant anatomical features unique to or especially relevant in raptors are emphasized here, without exhaustively reviewing general avian anatomy. Because raptors span several distantly related orders, notable intergroup differences in anatomy and physiology do exist.



The general external morphology of raptors is comparable to that of other flight-capable birds. Most species lack conspicuous sexual dimorphism, with a few notable exceptions (e.g., snowy owls, Andean condors, kestrels, peregrine falcons, merlins). In the majority of species, females are larger than males; the Cathartidae and Sagittaridae are exceptions to this pattern. The magnitude of weight differences between sexes varies and may be minimal in some groups (e.g., owls, Old World vultures).



All raptors possess feathers and are capable of flight. The cervical region of vultures is characteristically unfeathered, and several cathartid species possess skin folds on the neck that assist with thermoregulation. The feet and tarsi are bare in most raptors, though species inhabiting northern and arctic environments (e.g., golden eagles, rough-legged hawks) tend to have feathered tarsi. Most owls have feathered feet and tarsi, with the snowy owl's feathering extending distally to the digits. Wing feathers are classified as primaries (typically 10, numbered from medial to lateral—though falconers may use the reverse convention), secondaries (variable in number), and coverts. The tail generally comprises 12 feathers; the two central rectrices (sometimes called deck feathers) are usually slightly longer than the rest.



Wing and tail morphology reflect species-specific flight ecology. Falcons exhibit high wing loading (a high body weight to wing area ratio) and pointed wingtips, adaptations for powerful flapping flight and rapid speeds. In contrast, raptors with lower wing loading tend toward rounded wingtips, particularly in soaring specialists such as vultures. The degree of primary feather emargination correlates with soaring ability and is most pronounced in vultures. Tail length is associated with maneuverability; it tends to be longer in forest-dwelling species (e.g., accipiters) and shorter in soaring species (e.g., vultures). Owls possess specialized feather modifications that facilitate slow, virtually silent flight by minimizing air turbulence and feather-to-feather friction. These modifications include serrations on the leading edge of the outermost primaries, downy fringes on the trailing edges of flight and contour feathers, soft plumulaceous feathers on the wings and legs, and elongated pennula on the distal barbules in feather friction zones. In addition, owls have highly specialized feathers in their facial disc that function to channel sound toward the ears, a feature particularly well developed in great grey owls and barn owls.



Like all birds, raptors undergo periodic replacement of their feathers in species-specific patterns. In general, small to medium-sized species and most falconids replace their entire plumage within a single annual cycle, whereas larger species may require two to three years to complete a full molt. Exceptions exist: Tytonidae typically molt over a two-year cycle, and some Strigidae replace all tail feathers simultaneously (e.g., barred owls). Falconers and rehabilitation facilities routinely collect molted feathers in the event that imping of damaged feathers becomes necessary. Disease, nutritional deficits, or insufficient food availability can delay or interrupt normal molting. Most raptors exhibit distinct juvenile and adult plumage patterns, though males and females usually share a similar appearance, with several exceptions (e.g., snowy owls, kestrels, Northern harriers, peregrine falcons, merlins). Some species display variable color morphs (e.g., gyrfalcons, Eurasian buzzards, Eastern screech owls, ferruginous hawks, Swainson's hawks, rough-legged hawks) and recognizable subspecies (e.g., great horned owls, peregrine falcons). In free-ranging birds, especially juveniles requiring more than one year to achieve adult plumage, age can be approximated by examining the molting pattern of the remiges.



Raptors are readily recognized by the hooked tip of their beak. Beak shape and size are strongly influenced by the dietary habits and prey preferences of each species. The cere, a fleshy covering over the dorsal base of the beak that is perforated by the nostrils, is typically yellow to orange in adult birds and blue to green in juveniles. Falcons possess a tomial tooth on the upper beak (rhinotheca), with the exception of forest falcons (Micrastur spp.) and caracaras (Caracara spp.). Fish-eating eagles and eagles in general tend to have proportionally large beaks. The Cathartidae have perforate (open) nares situated just behind the beak, and falcons have the rostral nasal concha visible externally (distinct from the operculum found in parrots). The anatomical placement of the eyes in raptors provides binocular, forward-directed vision. Most raptors have a prominent supraorbital (superciliary) ridge above the eyes; the osprey is a notable exception in which this bony ridge is absent. Owl eyes are substantially larger relative to body size than those of other raptors (discussed further below). The secretary bird possesses modified feathers on the eyelid margins that resemble eyelashes. In older individuals of some raptor species, the iris may develop brown or dark speckles (e.g., bald eagles, snowy owls), while in other species the iris tends to darken with age (e.g., deep orange in adult Northern goshawks). The external ear openings are located caudal to the eyes and are typically concealed by specialized feathers, as in other birds. In owls, an operculum overlies the external ear opening, and the lateral wall of the ocular globe forms the medial boundary of the external auditory canal. Owls also possess asymmetric ear openings, a feature produced by soft tissue differences in most species or by bony asymmetry in some (e.g., barn owls, great grey owls), which enhances their ability to localize sound.



The uropygial (preen) gland is situated dorsally at the tail base and is present in all raptor species.



Raptor legs are generally longer in species that are active hunters, particularly those that prey on birds (e.g., accipiters), snakes, or fish (e.g., ospreys). Snake-eating species may additionally have thickened, heavily scaled tarsi. The secretary bird has exceptionally long legs and a reduced hallux (a cursorial foot type). Species that probe cavities, such as the African harrier-hawk (Polyboroides typus), possess tibiotarsal joints with an exceptionally wide range of motion. The sharp, curved claws (talons) at the tips of the digits are a defining feature of raptors; the talons of digit 1 (hallux) and digit 2 are the largest. Most raptors have an anisodactyl foot arrangement, but some species—including owls and the osprey—display zygodactyl or facultative zygodactyl configurations. Barn owls are distinctive in having a pectinate (comb-like) claw on the middle toe. The plantar surface of each toe bears multiple foot pads, including a central metatarsal pad and one digital pad per phalanx. In fish-eating species, particularly the osprey and certain piscivorous owls, the plantar surface is equipped with sharp spicules that aid in gripping slippery prey.
