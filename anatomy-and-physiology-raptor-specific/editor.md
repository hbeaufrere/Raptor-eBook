# Flight-related anatomy

A working knowledge of the principal muscle groups and skeletal anatomy—particularly the long bones—is essential for clinicians treating raptors, given the high prevalence of traumatic injuries and bone fractures in this patient population. Standard avian anatomy references provide comprehensive descriptions of the musculoskeletal system. Clinically relevant features in raptors include the presence of protective bony and fibrous structures around the strong flexor and extensor tendons of the feet: most species possess a bony supratendinal bridge at the distal tibiotarsus and deep flexor grooves on the caudal tarsometatarsus, often with associated sesamoid bones. Unlike many other avian species, most long bones in raptors are not pneumatized, with the exception of the femur and humerus. Cathartiformes exhibit more extensive long bone pneumatization, including the ulna; therefore, the ulna should not be used as a site for intraosseous catheterization in New World vultures. In certain Strigidae species, the femur may lack pneumatization.



Falcons have an additional sesamoid bone on the plantar aspect of the metatarsophalangeal joint, associated with the flexor tendon. Metatarsal bone 1 is positioned proximal to the hallux in eagles and some strigid species. In several raptor groups, certain tendons may be calcified, including the flexor and extensor tendons of the pelvic limb in Strigidae and the long extensor tendon of the major digit in falcons. Noteworthy features of the raptor wing skeleton include the presence of a sesamoid bone (os prominens) at the insertion of the tensor propatagialis on the wrist in Strigiformes. Some accipitrid species (e.g., Buteo spp., Accipiter spp.) also possess an os prominens, though it is usually smaller than in owls. Strigidae additionally have a humeroscapular bone visible on craniocaudal radiographic views of the shoulder. Falcons possess three sesamoid bones associated with the phalanges in the distal wing.



The muscular anatomy and surgical approaches for orthopedic repair in birds have been thoroughly described in standard references. The major flight muscles in raptors are analogous to those of other volant birds: the pectoral muscles power the downstroke, while the supracoracoid muscle drives the upstroke. Of particular functional importance in raptors is the digital flexor tendon system, which incorporates a locking mechanism between the major flexor tendon and its sheath that enables the powerful grip necessary for capturing and holding prey. The detailed tendon anatomy of raptors has been extensively reviewed elsewhere and is beyond the scope of this overview.
