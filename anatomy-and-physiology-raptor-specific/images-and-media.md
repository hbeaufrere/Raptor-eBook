# Species-specific adaptations

## Digestive System

All raptors are obligate meat eaters, though individual species may specialize as carnivores, piscivores, or insectivores, with most favoring one dietary category. The tongue tip is typically keratinized and paler than the surrounding oral mucosa, and caudally directed papillae at the tongue base facilitate swallowing. Among raptors, the Falconiformes, Accipitriformes (with the exception of the bearded vulture), and Cathartiformes possess a crop, whereas Strigiformes do not. The stomach consists of the proventriculus and ventriculus, both of which are distensible. Gastric acidity is lower in Strigiformes, and as a result, owls do not digest bones. All raptors form pellets (also called casts) composed of indigestible prey components that are molded in the ventriculus and regurgitated. Most raptors lack ceca, but Strigiformes possess a pair of well-developed, elongated ceca. Many raptor species have a gallbladder, and their cloacal anatomy follows the general avian pattern.



## Respiratory System

Species-specific nasal features in raptors were addressed in the external anatomy section above. The respiratory system otherwise conforms to the standard avian plan, with parabronchial lungs and an air sac system comprising nine air sacs. The glottis and syrinx are relatively simple structures, and the tracheal rings are complete. The turkey vulture, and possibly other cathartiform species, has large subcutaneous air sac diverticula that can expand dramatically during inhalant anesthesia.



## Urogenital System

The renal anatomy and physiology of raptors is broadly comparable to that of other birds and has been described in detail in other publications. Some raptor species, notably falcons, possess functional salt glands, although this is relatively uncommon.



In contrast to most avian species, a right ovary is present in many raptor species, including members of Falconidae, certain Accipitridae (e.g., Accipiter spp., Eurasian buzzards), some Strigiformes (e.g., boreal owls, northern saw-whet owls, long-eared owls), and turkey vultures. In hawks (e.g., red-tailed hawks) and eagles, the right ovary, when present, is typically rudimentary or absent. As in other birds, the oviduct is divided into five segments: infundibulum, magnum, isthmus, uterus, and vagina. Artificial insemination is widely practiced in captive raptor breeding programs, particularly for falcons. A range of interspecific hybrids can be produced using this technique. The most commonly bred hybrids involve falcon species, especially crosses using gyrfalcons because of their large body size (gyrfalcon x saker, gyrfalcon x peregrine, gyrfalcon x lanner).



## Cardiovascular System

Falconidae possess a proportionally larger heart than Accipitridae; this difference may lead to a misdiagnosis of cardiomegaly when interpreting radiographic images of falcons using accipitrid reference values. The Andean condor exhibits additional waveform deflections (R' and U waves) on its normal electrocardiogram that are not observed in other raptor species. Strigiformes have evolved specialized vascular adaptations in the cervical region that maintain cerebral blood flow despite the extreme rotational range of motion of the head. Beyond these features, the cardiovascular anatomy of raptors does not differ substantially from that of other birds.
