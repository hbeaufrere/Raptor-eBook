# Other Guidelines and Standards

The following are not laws or regulations, but widely accepted professional recommendations, guidelines, and institutional standards that govern the care, housing, and management of raptors in rehabilitation, educational, and sanctuary settings. While not legally binding in the same way as federal or state statutes, many of these standards are referenced by permitting agencies, required by accrediting bodies, or adopted as conditions of institutional animal use protocols. Adherence to these guidelines represents the current standard of care in the field.



### UC Davis Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC)

The UC Davis Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) oversees all activities involving live vertebrate animals at the university, including teaching, research, and clinical work conducted at the Veterinary Medical Teaching Hospital (VMTH) and affiliated wildlife rehabilitation programs. Any protocol involving raptors — whether for clinical trials, student training with live birds, or rehabilitation research — must be reviewed and approved by the IACUC prior to commencement. The committee ensures compliance with the Animal Welfare Act, the Public Health Service Policy on Humane Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, and the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals. IACUC approval is a prerequisite for obtaining or maintaining federal and state permits involving raptors at UC Davis facilities.

Official resource: https://research.ucdavis.edu/research-support/animal-care-use/iacuc/



### NWRA Minimum Standards for Wildlife Rehabilitation (3rd Edition)

Published by the National Wildlife Rehabilitators Association (NWRA), the Minimum Standards for Wildlife Rehabilitation (3rd Edition) is the primary professional reference establishing baseline care standards for wildlife rehabilitation in North America. The document covers biology, medicine, behavior, natural history, and rehabilitation practices applicable to all native wildlife, including raptors. It addresses facility design, caging requirements, nutrition, medical treatment protocols, release criteria, and record-keeping. USFWS and many state agencies reference these standards when issuing and evaluating rehabilitation permits, and adherence to NWRA standards is generally expected of all permitted rehabilitators. The standards are relevant at every level of rehabilitation experience and are frequently cited in permit conditions.

Official resource: https://www.nwrawildlife.org/page/Rehabilitator\_Resources



### AZA Owl Care Manual (2022)

The Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA) Owl Care Manual (2022) is a comprehensive, peer-reviewed guide compiled by recognized experts in avian management and zoological medicine. It covers all aspects of owl husbandry including housing and enclosure design, nutrition, veterinary care, behavioral management, reproduction, and conservation education. While primarily intended for AZA-accredited institutions, the manual is an invaluable reference for anyone involved in the long-term care of non-releasable owls in educational or sanctuary settings. AZA-accredited institutions are required to have copies of all relevant Animal Care Manuals for species in their collection. The manual also provides guidance on enrichment, handling, and transport that is directly applicable to raptor rehabilitation facilities holding education birds.

Official resource: https://www.aza.org/animal-care-manuals



### GFAS Standards for Birds of Prey Sanctuaries

The Global Federation of Animal Sanctuaries (GFAS) is the only globally recognized accreditation body for animal sanctuaries, rescue centers, and rehabilitation facilities. GFAS publishes species-specific Standards of Excellence that facilities must meet to achieve accreditation or verification. Their standards for birds of prey sanctuaries address enclosure size and design, perching, flight space, diet and nutrition, veterinary care, enrichment, staffing, and record-keeping specific to raptors held in permanent captive settings. GFAS accreditation signals to donors, regulators, and the public that a facility meets the highest ethical and care standards. Facilities that house non-releasable raptors long-term, whether as sanctuaries or as part of education programs, may pursue GFAS accreditation to demonstrate compliance with best practices.

Official resource: https://sanctuaryfederation.org/accreditation/



### IWRC Standards for Wildlife Rehabilitation

The International Wildlife Rehabilitation Council (IWRC) is a global organization dedicated to promoting excellence in wildlife rehabilitation. IWRC provides training, certification, and professional development resources for wildlife rehabilitators worldwide. Their guidelines and best practices cover intake procedures, triage, medical assessment, caging standards, nutrition, release criteria, and record-keeping for all native wildlife species including raptors. IWRC also administers the Certified Wildlife Rehabilitator (CWR) credential, which is increasingly recognized by state and federal agencies as evidence of professional competency. The organization's resources complement the NWRA minimum standards and are particularly useful for rehabilitators seeking formal certification.

Official resource: https://theiwrc.org/



### AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals

The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals is the nationally recognized standard for humane euthanasia methods across all animal species, including raptors and other birds. The guidelines provide detailed recommendations on acceptable methods, agents, and procedures for euthanasia in clinical, research, and field settings. For raptor rehabilitation, these guidelines are directly applicable when euthanasia is determined to be in the best interest of a non-releasable bird with a poor quality-of-life prognosis. IACUC protocols, rehabilitation permits, and veterinary practice standards all reference the AVMA euthanasia guidelines. Rehabilitation veterinarians and permitted rehabilitators should be familiar with the avian-specific sections of this document.

Official resource: https://www.avma.org/resources-tools/avma-policies/avma-guidelines-euthanasia-animals



### USGS Bird Banding Laboratory

The USGS Bird Banding Laboratory (BBL) publishes the North American Bird Banding Manual, which provides standardized protocols for the capture, banding, marking, and data reporting of wild birds, including raptors. Federal banding permits (issued under 50 CFR Parts 10, 13, and 21) require that all banding activities follow BBL protocols. The manual covers band selection and sizing for raptors, proper band placement, auxiliary marker use, data recording, and reporting requirements. Rehabilitation facilities that band raptors prior to release must comply with these standards, and all banding data must be submitted to the BBL through the Bandit reporting system. The manual is essential for any facility or individual holding a federal banding permit.

Official resource: https://www.usgs.gov/centers/eesc/science/bird-banding-laboratory
