# Federal

The possession, rehabilitation, and exhibition of raptors in the United States is governed by a complex framework of federal regulations. Because all native raptors are protected under the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA)** and, in the case of eagles, the **Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (BGEPA)**, any individual or facility handling these birds must hold the appropriate federal permits. The following is an overview of the key federal regulations relevant to raptor rehabilitation, education, and captive management.

### USFWS 50 CFR 21.76 – Migratory Bird Rehabilitation Permit

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Part 21_

This regulation establishes the federal permit required for the rehabilitation of injured, sick, or orphaned migratory birds, including all native raptors. The permit is issued by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) and authorizes permitted individuals and facilities to temporarily possess migratory birds for the purpose of treating and conditioning them for release back into the wild. Permit holders must meet minimum facility standards, maintain records of all birds received, and comply with disposition requirements (release, transfer, or euthanasia). Rehabilitators may not use birds in their care for educational purposes unless they also hold a separate education permit. The permit must be renewed periodically, and the USFWS may inspect facilities at any time.

### USFWS 50 CFR 21.95 – Special Purpose Possession: Education

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Part 21_

This section of the federal regulations authorizes the possession of live or dead migratory birds, including raptors, for educational purposes. Facilities such as nature centers, universities, and raptor centers may apply for this permit to use non-releasable birds in public education programs, school presentations, and interpretive displays. The permit specifies which species and how many individuals may be held, and it requires that the educational use genuinely serves conservation awareness. Dead specimens, feathers, and other parts may also be possessed under this permit for use in educational settings. Holders must maintain detailed records and ensure that birds are housed in appropriate conditions that meet welfare standards.

Official resource: [https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.95](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.95)

### USFWS 50 CFR 21.95 – Renesting

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Part 21_

Under the special purpose provisions of 50 CFR 21.95, permitted rehabilitators may return young raptors to active nests of the same species (renesting or fostering) as an alternative to hand-rearing in captivity. This practice is considered a preferred rehabilitation strategy for nestlings and young fledglings because it maximizes natural behavioral development, avoids human imprinting, and improves post-release survival. Renesting must be performed by or under the guidance of permitted individuals, and the process requires careful assessment of the foster nest, including confirmation that the adults are actively tending the nest and that the addition of a chick will not exceed the adults' capacity to provision. Documentation and reporting of renesting outcomes are expected under the permit.

Official resource: [https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.95](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.95)

### USFWS 50 CFR 22.50 – Eagle Exhibition

_Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (BGEPA), 16 USC §668, Title 50 CFR Part 22_

Bald and golden eagles receive additional protection under the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (BGEPA), separate from the MBTA. Under 50 CFR 22.50, a specific federal permit is required to possess live eagles for exhibition or educational purposes. This permit is distinct from the general migratory bird education permit and carries additional requirements due to the heightened legal protections afforded to eagles. Applicants must demonstrate adequate facilities, trained personnel, and a defined educational program. Eagles held under this permit may be displayed to the public or used in formal educational presentations, but may not be used for falconry or commercial purposes. The USFWS closely monitors eagle permits and may impose specific conditions regarding housing, veterinary care, and public display protocols.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-22/subpart-C/section-22.50

### USDA Animal Welfare Act – Exhibitor License

_Animal Welfare Act (AWA), 7 USC §2131_

The U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), through the Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS), administers the Animal Welfare Act (AWA), which regulates the exhibition of warm-blooded animals to the public. Facilities that display raptors to the public, including raptor centers, nature centers, and educational programs that charge admission or receive compensation, may be required to obtain a USDA exhibitor license. The AWA sets minimum standards for housing, feeding, veterinary care, and handling of exhibited animals. Licensed facilities are subject to unannounced USDA inspections. Notably, the AWA requirements apply in addition to USFWS permits, meaning that raptor education facilities may need to comply with both sets of regulations simultaneously. Some exemptions may apply for certain nonprofit or governmental organizations, but the applicability should be verified on a case-by-case basis.

Official resource: https://www.aphis.usda.gov/animal-welfare

### USGS 50 CFR 10, 13, 21 – Federal Bird Banding Permit and NWRA Minimum Standards

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Parts 10, 13, 21_

Federal bird banding is administered by the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) Bird Banding Laboratory under the authority of 50 CFR Parts 10, 13, and 21. A federal banding permit authorizes the capture, marking, and release of wild birds using uniquely numbered metal leg bands for scientific and management purposes. In the context of raptor rehabilitation, banding is important for tracking individual birds post-release and contributing data on survival, movement, and population dynamics. Rehabilitators who wish to band birds prior to release must either hold a federal banding permit themselves or coordinate with a licensed bander. The National Wildlife Rehabilitators Association (NWRA) has established minimum standards for wildlife rehabilitation that are widely referenced by federal and state agencies when evaluating permit applications and facility compliance. These standards address facility design, veterinary care protocols, caging requirements, diet, record-keeping, and ethical considerations for euthanasia and release decision-making. ### Endangered Species Act – 50 CFR Part 17

_Endangered Species Act (ESA), 16 USC §1531–1544, Title 50 CFR Part 17_

The Endangered Species Act (ESA), passed in 1973, prohibits the "take" of threatened and endangered plant and animal species and their parts or products. "Take" is broadly defined to include harassing, harming, pursuing, hunting, shooting, wounding, killing, trapping, capturing, or collecting. Several raptor species in California carry federal threatened or endangered status, including the California condor. Permitted wildlife rehabilitators and veterinarians may legally treat and, when necessary, euthanize endangered species. However, the USFWS Migratory Bird Permit Office must be notified within 24 hours upon receiving a threatened or endangered raptor. Ideally, permission is obtained prior to euthanasia of such species, though euthanasia may proceed without prior approval when necessary for humane welfare reasons. Rehabilitators should also notify the permit office prior to releasing a threatened or endangered bird to ensure compliance with species-specific release requirements.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-17

### CITES – Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species – 50 CFR Part 23

_Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora, Title 50 CFR Part 23_

CITES is an international agreement established in 1975 among over 175 nations to prevent the endangerment or extinction of species due to international trade. Under CITES and its implementing regulations at 50 CFR Part 23, listed species — whether live, dead, or their parts or products (including biological samples such as blood) — cannot be imported or exported without a permit. Species are categorized in Appendices I, II, or III according to their level of conservation concern. Many raptor species are CITES-listed, meaning that any international transport of raptors or raptor specimens requires CITES permits in addition to other applicable federal and state authorizations. This regulation is particularly relevant to rehabilitation facilities and research institutions that may need to ship biological samples across international borders for diagnostic or research purposes.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-23

### Lacey Act – 50 CFR Part 16

_Lacey Act, 16 USC 3371–3378, 18 USC §42–43, Title 50 CFR Part 16_

The Lacey Act, passed in 1900, is the oldest U.S. law protecting wildlife. It prohibits the sale, acquisition, and interstate or international transport of any fish, wildlife, or plants — or their parts or products — that were obtained in violation of any U.S. treaty, regulation, or Native American tribal law. The Act also requires that live wildlife being transported into the United States be kept under humane conditions. A 1981 amendment, known as the Injurious Wildlife Act (50 CFR Part 16), further prevents the importation or interstate transport of species deemed injurious to human health, agriculture, or native wildlife, though exceptions can be made by permit for zoo collections or scientific, medicinal, and educational purposes. For raptor rehabilitation and falconry, the Lacey Act reinforces the requirement that all birds be legally acquired and documented, and that interstate transport of raptors comply with all applicable permit conditions.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-16

### Wild Bird Conservation Act – 50 CFR Part 15

_Wild Bird Conservation Act, Title 50 CFR Part 15_

While not directly applicable to U.S.-native raptors, the Wild Bird Conservation Act (signed into law in 1992) is an important statute that limits or prohibits the importation of exotic bird species into the United States. The Act is designed to ensure that all U.S. trade in exotic birds is sustainable, and it established the Exotic Bird Conservation Fund to support conservation programs in countries of origin. This law is relevant to facilities that may house non-native raptor species (for example, in zoological or educational collections) and to falconers who may possess non-native species, as importation of such birds requires compliance with the Act's provisions.



### USFWS 50 CFR 21.82 – Falconry Standards and Falconry Permitting

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Part 21_

Falconry — the practice of using trained raptors to hunt wild quarry — is regulated under 50 CFR 21.82, which establishes federal standards and permitting requirements. Falconers must hold both federal and state falconry permits. There are three license levels: Apprentice, General, and Master, each with specific requirements for experience and restrictions on the number and species of birds that may be kept. Apprentice falconers must be sponsored by a General or Master falconer and may possess only one raptor at a time, limited to a red-tailed hawk or American kestrel. Falconers may trap wild raptors (generally hatch-year birds) or use captive-bred birds. The regulation includes detailed requirements for housing, care, banding, and reporting. Of particular relevance to rehabilitation, wildlife rehabilitators may enlist falconers to assist in reconditioning raptors for release without the bird counting toward the falconer's possession limit, provided the bird remains on the rehabilitator's permit. Veterinarians working with falconry clients should verify that all necessary permits are in place.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.82

### USFWS 50 CFR 21.73 – Scientific Collecting Permits

_Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA), 16 USC 703-712, Title 50 CFR Part 21_

A separate USFWS scientific collecting permit under 50 CFR 21.73 is required for individuals who collect biological samples from migratory birds and eagles for scientific or educational purposes. Collectible materials include blood, feathers, gastrointestinal contents, tissues, oral and cloacal swabs, and eggs. This permit is distinct from the rehabilitation permit and the education permit. However, certain exceptions exist: licensed migratory bird rehabilitators are permitted to bank feathers for the purpose of imping and to collect samples for scientific purposes during the normal course of rehabilitation without a separate scientific collecting permit, though additional permitting is required to do so with eagles. Licensed bird banders may also be exempt from the scientific collecting permit requirement in some circumstances. Researchers and academic institutions affiliated with raptor centers should verify their permit obligations when designing studies involving biological sample collection from wild raptors.

Official resource: https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-50/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-21/subpart-C/section-21.73
