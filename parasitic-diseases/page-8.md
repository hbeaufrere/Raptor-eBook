## Trichomoniasis

Trichomonas gallinae has a direct life cycle and does not form environmentally resistant cysts. Affected raptors typically present with caseous oral plaques, regurgitation, and weight loss. In severe cases, necrotic lesions may extend to involve the sinuses, skull, and occasionally the liver, lungs, and air sacs. Infection is usually acquired through consumption of infected prey (e.g., columbiform and passerine species), though direct transmission between raptors and parental feeding of nestlings are also recognized routes. Bird-eating species are consequently at greatest risk. Differential diagnoses for oral plaques in raptors include trichomoniasis, capillariasis, poxvirus, herpesvirus, bacterial stomatitis, neoplasia, and food debris. Diagnosis is confirmed by direct microscopic examination of fresh wet mounts prepared with warm saline. Nitroimidazole drugs are the standard treatment options. Prevention strategies include freezing prey items and performing regular oral examinations.



## Capillariasis

Capillariid nematodes of the genus Eucoleus infect the upper digestive tract, including the oropharynx, esophagus, and crop. Clinical signs are nonspecific and may include oral plaques, regurgitation, weight loss, inappetence, diarrhea, and rarely melena. Diagnosis is made by detecting the characteristic bipolar eggs on fecal examination, cytology, or histopathology. The life cycle is usually direct but may involve earthworms as intermediate hosts. Recommended anthelmintics include fenbendazole and ivermectin. Fenbendazole has been associated with bone marrow toxicity and leukopenia in certain avian species, including great horned owls, a harpy eagle, and accipitrid vultures. It is prudent to avoid its use during the molt and in vultures, and to monitor complete blood counts during prolonged treatment courses.



## External Parasitism

Infestations with chewing lice (Mallophaga) are commonly identified during examination of raptor patients. Because these ectoparasites feed on feathers and skin debris rather than blood, they are unaffected by systemic antiparasitic agents; topical pyrethrin or fipronil spray is the preferred treatment. Hippoboscid fly infestations are common in North American raptors, especially in young or immunocompromised individuals. These flies inhabit the plumage and external ear canals and may serve as mechanical vectors of West Nile virus and hemoparasites such as Haemoproteus spp.



## Hemoparasites

Most hemoparasites affecting raptors—including Haemoproteus spp., Leucocytozoon spp., and microfilariae—are considered nonpathogenic in immunocompetent adult birds. Plasmodium spp. are considered pathogenic in snowy owls, gyrfalcons, and possibly other species. In general, snowy owls appear to be more susceptible to hemoparasite-related disease, possibly as a consequence of their evolutionary origins in vector-poor arctic habitats. Clinical babesiosis has been described in several raptor species and has been treated successfully with imidocarb dipropionate at 5 mg/kg IM administered weekly for two treatments.
