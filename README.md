# Welcome

<figure><img src=".gitbook/assets/_MG_3089.jpg" alt="" width="563"><figcaption></figcaption></figure>

This ebook was developed as a **practical learning resource** for those involved in the medical care and rehabilitation of wild birds of prey in **California**. It is not intended to be a comprehensive reference, but rather a concise and focused guide providing essential information to support effective, evidence-based, and compassionate raptor care.

The content is tailored primarily for veterinary students and zoological medicine residents at the University of California, Davis, with particular emphasis on clinical procedures and species commonly encountered at the California Raptor Center (CRC). The material aims to complement hands-on experience by presenting key concepts, diagnostic approaches, and treatment considerations relevant to the practice of avian medicine.

### **Why treat and rehabilitate wild raptors?**

Raptors occupy the **top trophic levels of diverse ecosystems** and play a critical role in maintaining ecological balance across a wide range of habitats. However, they face numerous threats—**most of which are anthropogenic**—that frequently result in presentation to wildlife rehabilitation facilities such as the California Raptor Center.

The principal causes of morbidity and mortality in raptors include **trauma, toxin exposure, and infectious diseases**. As these species adapt to increasingly urbanized and fragmented landscapes, their proximity to human activity and infrastructure heightens the risk of **traumatic injury**, such as from collisions, electrocution, and entanglement. As apex predators, raptors are also vulnerable to bioaccumulation of **pesticides and environmental contaminants** through their prey, with exposures often linked to agricultural practices, pest control, pollution, and hunting-related lead ingestion. Large scale unintentional environmental toxicosis can have devastating effects on raptor populations with notable examples such as DDT and diclofenac. \
In addition, **intentional human persecution**, although illegal, still occurs sporadically and contributes to injury and mortality in both wild and rehabilitated populations. Finally, a variety of **infectious diseases** affect raptors, some associated with epizootic events partly influenced by climate and environmental changes, while others emerge in connection with temporary captivity during rehabilitation or long-term housing in educational and falconry settings.

While the overall population-level impact of raptor rehabilitation may be modest, caring for these birds remains a shared responsibility and ethical commitment, particularly since most injuries are human-induced. Rehabilitation therefore carries important animal welfare and moral dimensions. Beyond the direct benefit to individual birds, this work provides valuable opportunities for education and training in avian and raptor medicine, as well as for public outreach and fostering broader awareness of wildlife conservation issues. Moreover, advances in clinical care and research derived from rehabilitation efforts can significantly benefit threatened and endangered raptor species, where each individual represents a meaningful contribution to population recovery.
