## Mycobacteriosis

Mycobacteriosis has been reported to occur with higher frequency in wild raptors compared to other wild bird groups. In birds, mycobacteriosis is primarily a gastrointestinal and hepatic disease, most commonly caused by Mycobacterium avium subsp. avium and M. intracellulare. Clinical signs in affected raptors include chronic wasting, diarrhea, poor feather quality, and gastrointestinal signs. Diagnostic imaging may reveal hepatomegaly and splenomegaly. The preferred diagnostic approach is endoscopy-guided biopsy with histopathologic examination, PCR, and/or culture of lesional tissue. Treatment of wild birds is generally not recommended. Medical therapy typically involves a combination of antimycobacterial antibiotics and may extend over months to years.



## Bacterial Enteritis

Bacterial enteritis has been suspected in raptors presenting with diarrhea and malabsorption, with campylobacteriosis, salmonellosis, and colibacillosis described anecdotally. Raptor exposure to Salmonella spp. is often linked to contaminated food sources (e.g., poultry, rodents). The normal digestive flora of raptors remains incompletely characterized, but some Gram-negative and anaerobic bacteria are considered normal inhabitants. Clostridial gastrointestinal infections are commonly reported in captive falcons and golden eagles, often resulting in acute or subacute mortality. These infections are frequently attributed to toxin-producing Clostridium perfringens and may be associated with improperly thawed food. All raptor food items should be thawed under refrigeration.
