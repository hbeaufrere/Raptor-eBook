# California condor

### Description

The California Condor is the **largest soaring land bird in North America**, with a wingspan of approximately 2.8 m and a body weight of 7.0–9.9 kg. Adults are almost entirely black with striking white triangular patches on the underwing coverts, a conspicuous white bar on the upper wing formed by the tips of the greater coverts, and a prominent ruff of dark lanceolate feathers around the lower neck. The head and neck are largely bare; in adults, the head is bright orange to yellowish with purplish-red coloration on the lower foreneck and crop area. The bill is ivory with a brown basal band. Juveniles have a dark grayish-black head covered in gray down, duller brownish-black body plumage, and sooty underwing coverts with variable white fringing. **The head gradually transitions from dark gray to the bright orange and yellow of adults over 6–8 years**. Sexes are similar in plumage at all ages, but **males average slightly heavier** (mean 8.8 kg) than females (mean 8.1 kg).

### Distribution and Migration

The California Condor historically ranged across western North America, with Pleistocene fossils from Florida to New York suggesting a once much broader distribution that contracted following the late Pleistocene megafauna extinctions. By the early 20th century, the species was largely confined to southern California, with remnant populations in Oregon, northern Baja California, and scattered inland areas. By the mid-20th century, the range had contracted to a wishbone-shaped area surrounding the southern San Joaquin Valley in parts of San Benito, Monterey, Fresno, Kings, San Luis Obispo, Santa Barbara, Ventura, Los Angeles, Kern, and Tulare counties. **The species is non-migratory but covers enormous distances in daily foraging, with home ranges of hundreds of square kilometers.** By 1987, the last wild condors were captured for a captive breeding program. Since 1992, reintroduction efforts have returned birds to southern and central California, the Grand Canyon region of Arizona/Utah, and Baja California, Mexico. In California, reintroduced populations are currently centered in Ventura, Santa Barbara, San Luis Obispo, San Benito, and Monterey counties, with Big Sur being a key coastal release site. **These populations are not yet self-sustaining, primarily due to ongoing lead poisoning mortality**.

### Diet and Biology

The California Condor is an **exclusive carrion feeder**, primarily dependent on **large mammalian carcasses** including cattle, deer, sheep, ground squirrels, and coyotes. **Historically, marine mammals such as whales and sea lions were also important food sources**, and reintroduced coastal populations have resumed feeding on beached marine mammals. **Unlike Turkey Vultures, condors do not locate food by olfaction; instead, they rely on keen eyesight and observe other scavengers** such as Turkey Vultures, Golden Eagles, and Common Ravens to find carcasses. Condors are highly social feeders and dominate most other avian scavengers at food sources, except Golden Eagles. Lead poisoning from ingested ammunition fragments in carcasses is the primary mortality threat and the main obstacle to population recovery.

Condors nest in **natural cavities in cliffs and large trees, laying their eggs directly on the substrate with no constructed nest.** The invariable **clutch size is a single egg**, which is pale blue-green fading to white. Incubation lasts 53–60 days (mean 57 days) and is shared by both parents. Chicks are altricial, covered in white down at hatching that is replaced by gray down within several weeks. The fledging period is approximately 5–6 months, and young remain dependent on their parents for over a year after fledging, which limits breeding to every other year for successful pairs. **Sexual maturity is reached at 6–8 years of age**. The species is long-lived, with individuals potentially reaching 45–60 years or more in captivity.

### Subspecies

The California Condor is monotypic — no subspecies are recognized. No geographic or sex-specific variation in appearance or molt strategies has been reported.

### Molting

The California Condor exhibits a **Simple Basic Strategy** with incomplete prebasic molts and no preformative or prealternate molts. Flight feather molt follows a Staffelmauser (stepwise) pattern, in which incomplete molts produce 2–4 overlapping waves of feather replacement within the primaries and secondaries. This strategy results in multiple small gaps rather than a single large gap in the wing, which is adaptive for maintaining flight efficiency during molt. Primaries are replaced distally (p1 to p10), secondaries proximally from s1 and s5 and distally from the tertials, and rectrices generally distally. Most primary molt occurs between February and September, with longer feathers taking 3.5–4 months to regrow. Wild condors replaced an average of 4.4 of 8 emarginated primaries per wing per year. Individual Staffelmauser patterns are unique to each bird and were used for photo-identification of the remnant wild population. **Definitive Basic Plumage with full adult head coloration is typically attained at 6–8 years of age through a series of gradual transitions**.
