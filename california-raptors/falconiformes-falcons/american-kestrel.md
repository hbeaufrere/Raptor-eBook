# American kestrel

### Description

The American Kestrel (Falco sparverius) is the smallest falcon in North America and the most numerous and widespread falcon on the continent. Formerly known as the Sparrow Hawk, it belongs to the order Falconiformes and family Falconidae. Adults measure 22–31 cm (8.7–12.2 in) in total length with a wingspan of 51–61 cm (20–24 in).



The species is strongly sexually dichromatic and shows reversed sexual size dimorphism (females larger). Adult males have blue-gray wings, a rufous crown patch surrounded by blue-gray, a rufous back with black barring, and a rufous tail with a single broad subterminal black band and white tip. Two bold black vertical facial stripes ("mustache marks" or malar stripes) frame the white auricular region — a feature sometimes interpreted as false "eyespots" that may deter predators from behind. The breast is white to buff with variable black spotting. Adult females have rufous wings, back, and tail, all marked with dark brown barring throughout. The female's tail has multiple narrow black bands rather than the male's single subterminal band. Females have streaked underparts with rufous-brown tones and are generally less colorful overall. Both sexes have dark eyes, a yellow cere and eye-ring, and yellow to orange legs. There is considerable individual variation in plumage across the species' range.



Juveniles resemble adults of their respective sex but with more heavily streaked underparts. Juvenile males may show more brown in the wings than adults. The female is approximately 10% heavier than the male, weighing 100–170 g compared to the male's 80–143 g.



### Distribution and Migration

The American Kestrel has the most extensive range of any falcon in the Western Hemisphere, occurring from Alaska and northern Canada south to Tierra del Fuego, Argentina, including the Caribbean islands. In North America, it breeds throughout most of the continent from the boreal treeline south through Mexico. Northern populations (Canada and northern United States) are migratory, moving south in autumn to winter from the central United States through Central America. Birds breeding in the southern United States, Mexico, and further south are largely resident year-round. Migration is monitored at hawk watch sites across North America; the Hawk Migration Association of North America (HMANA) tracks kestrel flights at coastal and mountain sites. Peak autumn migration typically occurs in September and early October, with concentrations noted at sites such as Cape May (New Jersey) and the Goshute Mountains (Nevada).



In California, the American Kestrel is a common year-round resident throughout much of the state, found in open habitats from sea level to montane meadows. It breeds in all regions of California where suitable nesting cavities and open foraging habitat exist, including the Central Valley, coastal lowlands, foothills, and desert edges. California populations are largely resident, although some altitudinal migration occurs, with birds breeding at higher elevations moving to lower areas in winter. The species is supplemented in winter by migrants from the Pacific Northwest and interior West. In winter, kestrels are especially common in agricultural areas of the Central Valley and along roadsides throughout southern California. Differential habitat use between sexes has been documented in winter territories: females tend to occupy more open habitats while males use areas with more tree cover.



### Diet and Biology

American Kestrels feed primarily on insects and other invertebrates (grasshoppers, cicadas, beetles, dragonflies, scorpions, spiders), as well as small mammals (voles, mice, shrews), small birds, lizards, snakes, and occasionally bats and frogs. Diet composition varies seasonally and geographically: insects predominate in summer, while small mammals become more important in winter when insect availability declines. Some individuals become proficient at catching small birds and flying insects in flight.



Kestrels hunt from elevated perches (utility wires, fence posts, dead snags) or by hovering — facing into the wind with rapid wingbeats while scanning the ground below. Prey is seized with the feet on the ground or in mid-air, then carried to a perch for consumption. They are known to cache surplus prey in grass clumps, tree hollows, fence posts, and other concealed locations. Like other raptors, kestrels can detect ultraviolet light, which allows them to see the urine trails of voles and other small mammals.



The American Kestrel is a secondary cavity nester, relying on natural tree hollows, abandoned woodpecker holes, rock crevices, and niches in buildings and other structures. The male locates potential nest sites and presents them to the female, who makes the final selection. No nesting material is added; the female lays directly on the cavity floor. Clutch size is typically 4–5 eggs (range 3–7), white to light reddish-brown with fine brownish spotting. Incubation lasts 26–32 days and is shared by both parents, though the female does the majority of incubation. Nestlings are altricial and fledge at 28–31 days. Kestrels may raise 1–2 broods per season in some areas. Sexual maturity is reached at one year of age. Kestrels readily accept artificial nest boxes, which has made them a popular target for citizen science and conservation programs, including the American Kestrel Partnership (formerly coordinated by The Peregrine Fund, now merged with Cornell Lab's NestWatch).



### Subspecies

Up to 17 subspecies of the American Kestrel are recognized across its vast range in the Western Hemisphere, making it one of the most polytypic falcons. The two subspecies relevant to North America and California are:



F. s. sparverius — the nominate and most widespread subspecies, breeding across most of North America from Alaska and Canada south through the United States and into Mexico. This is the subspecies found in California. It is the largest of the North American subspecies.



F. s. paulus — a smaller, paler resident subspecies found in the southeastern United States (primarily Florida and the Gulf states), associated with longleaf pine sandhill habitats. This subspecies is of conservation concern due to habitat loss.



Other notable subspecies include F. s. peninsularis (Baja California and northwest Mexico), F. s. tropicalis (southern Mexico through northern Central America), and numerous Caribbean and South American forms (F. s. dominicensis, F. s. caribaearum, F. s. brevipennis, F. s. isabellinus, F. s. ochraceus, F. s. caucae, F. s. aequatorialis, F. s. peruvianus, F. s. fernandensis, F. s. cinnamominus, and F. s. cearae). Geographic variation is clinal in many plumage traits, with southern and island populations generally being smaller and more richly colored.



### Molting

American Kestrels undergo a complete prebasic molt annually, which follows the typical falcon molt pattern. The molt sequence in the flight feathers begins with the innermost primary (P1) and proceeds outward (descendantly) to P10. Secondary molt begins at multiple foci, typically starting at S1 (innermost), S5, and the tertials, then converging. Tail feathers molt begins centrally (R1) and proceeds outward.



In adults, molt begins in late spring or early summer (typically May–June), peaks during mid-summer, and is completed by late fall (September–November). The timing overlaps with the breeding season; females may begin molt during incubation, while males may initiate molt slightly earlier. Molt is typically suspended during peak nesting demands and migration, then completed on the wintering grounds or after migration in resident populations. The preformative molt in first-year birds is partial and includes body feathers and some wing coverts but not flight feathers. First-year birds acquire adult-like plumage with their first complete prebasic molt at approximately one year of age. In rehabilitation, molt can be delayed or disrupted by stress, poor nutrition, or captivity; adequate nutrition (especially protein and amino acids) is critical for healthy feather regrowth.
