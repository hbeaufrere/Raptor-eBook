# Diversity and Taxonomy

## Global raptor diversity

All raptors, or birds of prey, look somewhat similar and are all characterized by their strict carnivorous diet, their ability to hunt prey, their large claws or talons, their hooked bills, and their generally acute senses adapted to finding preys or carrions. Through **convergent evolution**, they have all acquired comparable hunting adaptations, but the various groups of raptors are indeed very different from each other.

**Raptors are not a distinct taxonomic entity** and raptor groups do not necessarily share close phylogenetic relationships. As such, most raptorial orders are not closely related.

Interestingly, some raptor groups are more related to other birds than to each other. For instance, **owls are more closely related to mousebirds (Coliiformes) and falcons are closer to parrots (Psittaciformes)**. On the other side, accipitriformes and cathartiformes are sister groups. In addition, **New-world and Old-world vultures are unrelated**. Some birds not traditionally considered raptors could still fit this description such as shrikes, seriemas, ravens, or skuas. Conversely, not all raptors specialize in hunting vertebrate preys, and many exhibit dietary habits that parallel those of other avian groups. Some species primarily consume insects (such as kestrels and certain kites), resembling the feeding ecology of many passerines or insectivorous birds. Others feed mainly on mollusks (as seen in snail kites) or are piscivorous (like the osprey), similar to herons and other fish-eating waterbirds. **Vultures are highly specialized scavengers** and represent the only terrestrial vertebrates that have fully adapted to this unique trophic niche, which requires the ability to efficiently search vast areas while minimizing energy expenditure. With that said, some vultures occasionally predate on weak animals or neonates such as black vultures. In addition, while we typically picture raptors as large birds, many are indeed small like kestrels, merlins, or sharp-shinned hawks.&#x20;

We also typically separate raptors into **diurnal raptors** (accipitriformes, cathartiformes, falconiformes) and **nocturnal raptors** (strigiformes). While this division is practical and generally true, many exception occurs such as some owls being mainly diurnal like Northern hawk and snowy owls and at least one hawk being mainly crepuscular, the bat hawk, an African raptor species.&#x20;

Overall, this shows that the term "raptor" represents a functional and conventional group and does not have a unified definiion, even though most people will recognize raptors as raptors. While this category remains useful from a clinical and ecological standpoint, most medical and physiological principles applied to raptors are not unique to them and are shared across many avian species. As such, learning general avian medicine is very useful to apply to raptor care.

## Raptors in California

Raptors are widespread throughout California, but their species composition and distribution vary considerably among the state’s highly diverse habitats—from coastal cliffs and deserts to alpine forests and grasslands. Excluding rare vagrants, approximately **23 species of diurnal raptors (hawks, eagles, falcons, and vultures) and 13 species of nocturnal raptors (owls) occur in California.**

Occasionally, northern or irruptive species, such as the Snowy Owl and Northern Hawk Owl, are observed in northern parts of the state during exceptional winter influxes, but these occurrences are rare and unpredictable.

California also serves as a major migration corridor for raptors, particularly along the Pacific Flyway. Each spring and fall, thousands of birds move through key migration sites such as, locally, the **Marin headlands (particularly Hawk Hill, seen below) or the Yolo bypass wildlife area**.

Many species are declining in population numbers in California, but two species are critically endangered and subject to specific and increased conservation efforts. They are listed as both state endangered species by the California Department of Fish and Wildlife (CDFW) and federally endangered and are the **Spotted owl and the California condor**. In addition, other state-listed endangered or threatened species in California include the Swainson's hawk, the bald eagle, the elf owl, and the great gray owl.&#x20;

The California Raptor Center mainly sees birds from the northern Central Valley that lives in **oak Savannah or riparian habitats** in addition to a lower proportion of birds as a result of migrations or transfers from other areas of California.&#x20;

As such, **8 species of diurnal birds and 3 species of nocturnal birds are regularly seen**. Some are year-round occurence such as red-tailed hawks or white-tailed kites and some are strictly seasonal such as Swainson's hawks. While some raptors are permanent occurrence in the area, their population may vary with the addition of migrants from more northern latitudes or closeby moutainous areas in the Winter such as red-tailed hawks and northern harriers or from more southern latitudes in the Summer such as white-tailed kites. Some raptor species, although regularly occurring in northern California, are seldom encountered in the Central Valley and are therefore uncommonly treated at the California Raptor Center. These include primarily forest-associated species such as the Northern Goshawk, which favors high elevation dense coniferous habitats of the Sierra Nevada; Golden Eagles and Bald Eagles, which nest mainly in foothill and montane areas and occur only sporadically in the Valley lowlands; and species with restricted geographic ranges such as the Spotted Owl, typically confined to mature forests of the Sierra Nevada and coastal redwoods. As a result, most raptors admitted to the CRC represent open-habitat or generalist species adapted to the grasslands and agricultural landscapes typical of the Central Valley, but also some associated with urbanized environments and riparian habitats (mainly the Sacramento river, American river, and Putah creek). **From a conservation perspective, the white-tailed kite and the Swainson's hawk are the species of highest conservation concern that are routinely presented to the California Raptor Center.**<br>
