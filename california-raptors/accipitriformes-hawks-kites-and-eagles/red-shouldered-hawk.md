# Red-shouldered hawk

## Description

### General Appearance

The Red-shouldered Hawk is a medium-sized, relatively slender buteo with a **proportionally long tail** and broad, rounded wings. It is intermediate in size between the smaller Broad-winged Hawk (_B. platypterus_) and the larger Red-tailed Hawk (_B. jamaicensis_). **In soaring flight, the wings are held flat** and the tail is fanned. In active flight, the species often adopts a **rapid, accipiter-like flapping pattern alternating with short glides — a distinctive flight style among North American buteos** that can cause confusion with Cooper's Hawks. **When backlit in flight, a translucent crescent-shaped panel near the base of the outer primaries is a reliable field mark** at all ages (white in adults, tawny in immatures).

The species is named for the rufous lesser upperwing coverts, which form conspicuous "red shoulder" patches visible on perched birds. The flight feathers are **boldly barred black and white** on the dorsal surface, and the tail is relatively long for a buteo, marked by several wide dark bars with intervening narrow white bars and a white tip. Unlike the Swainson's Hawk, the Red-shouldered Hawk is **not polymorphic**, though plumage saturation varies geographically across subspecies.

### Adult Plumage

The head is medium brown with tawny streaking and a predominantly white chin. Occipital feathers are white-tipped and dark. The upperparts are mostly dark brown with rufous feather edgings. The underparts are heavily barred rufous, thinning toward the lower abdomen, with white to buffy feather bases. From below, the wing appears distinctly two-toned: rufous underwing coverts contrast sharply with the black-and-white barred primaries and secondaries. The lesser upperwing coverts are solidly rufous, forming the diagnostic shoulder patch. Secondaries and inner primaries are tipped white. Leg feathers are creamy with rufous barring. The tail is black with three to four narrow white bars.

**Western adults (**_**elegans**_**) have the brightest rufous underparts of any subspecies**, with solid rufous bibs, brighter rufous shoulder patches, wider white tail bands, and paler heads than eastern birds. Southern Florida adults (_extimus_) are the palest, with grayish or whitish heads and pale gray upper backs.

### Juvenile Plumage

Juveniles are brown above with varying amounts of tawny feather edgings. The underparts are whitish to buffy, with elongated or oval dark brown spots largest on the breast and sides, spaced somewhat sparingly. Undertail coverts are largely white, and flank and leg feathers are streaked rather than barred. The tail is dark brown above with many fine light brown bands and a narrow white tip; an occasional rufous wash may be present on the basal half. The underwing is uniformly white to cream-colored, lacking the rufous coverts of the adult. The translucent wing crescent is tawny rather than white. Juvenile plumage is worn for approximately 18 months.

**Western juveniles (**_**elegans**_**) are distinctive and considerably more adult-like than their eastern counterparts, with underparts more barred than streaked**, rufous wing coverts, pied flight and tail feathers, and a white (rather than tawny) underwing crescent. Eastern juveniles are superficially similar to juvenile Broad-winged Hawks but are best separated by shape (longer tail, more rounded wingtips), flight action (accipiter-like flapping), and the crescent-shaped translucent wing panel.

### Differences Between Sexes

Males and females are alike in plumage pattern, exhibiting the reverse sexual dimorphism (RSD) typical of Accipitridae: **females average approximately 700 g versus 550 g in males** — roughly 27% heavier. Eastern males may have slightly grayer periocular plumage, while females tend to be browner in this area. Female vocalizations are noticeably lower-pitched than those of males. Size dimorphism is most evident in mass and wing chord rather than in coloration.

### Bare Parts

The bill is black at all ages. **The iris progresses from pale to medium gray-brown in immatures to dark brown in adults**. The cere and bare facial skin are pale at hatching, greenish yellow in immatures, and bright yellow in adults. Legs and feet follow a similar progression from pale yellow or greenish in immatures to bright yellow in adults; talons are black at all ages.

### Subspecies

Four to five subspecies are recognized, varying primarily in size (decreasing from north to south and east to west) and plumage saturation. Distribution boundaries between some forms remain poorly defined.

**B. l. lineatus** — The nominate and largest subspecies. Breeds from the southern Maritime Provinces across to southern Ontario, south to North Carolina and Tennessee, and west to central Minnesota, eastern Iowa, southeastern Kansas, and eastern Missouri. Generally the darkest in adult plumage. Northern adults tend to have dark streaks on breast and belly feathers.

**B. l. alleni** — Breeds south of _lineatus_, from North Carolina, Tennessee, Arkansas, and Oklahoma south to Louisiana, eastern Texas, and the northern half of Florida. Smaller than the nominate form, sometimes with a paler, gray head and back. Adults lack the dark breast and belly feather streaks found in _lineatus_. Juveniles are more heavily streaked and somewhat barred below; leg feathers and flanks are usually barred; the tail has little or no rufous wash at the base.

**B. l. texanus** — Southeastern Texas. Considered by some authorities as part of _alleni_ rather than a distinct subspecies.

**B. l. extimus** — Breeds in the southern half of Florida. The smallest and palest subspecies, with a pale grayish or whitish head, pale gray upper back, and pale to medium rufous barring on underparts. Juveniles show a white (rather than tawny) dorsal wing panel.

**B. l. elegans** — Breeds in California west of the Sierra Nevada, in Oregon west of the Cascade Range, and into northwestern Baja California, Mexico. Adults have the brightest rufous underparts with solid bibs, brighter shoulder patches, and wider white tail bands. Juveniles are more similar to adults than in any other subspecies. This population has recently expanded northward into Oregon and eastward into Arizona. Genetic analyses (complete mitochondrial ND2 sequencing) indicate deep divergence from eastern populations, with no shared mitochondrial haplotypes and an estimated isolation period exceeding 100,000 years. **Some authorities have proposed treating&#x20;**_**elegans**_**&#x20;as a full species**.

## Distribution and Migration

### Overall Range

The Red-shouldered Hawk has a disjunct distribution, with an **extensive eastern population and a geographically separated western population approximately 1,600 km apart**.

The eastern population ranges from southern New Brunswick and western Ontario south through the eastern United States to Florida, the Gulf Coast, and eastern Mexico (Veracruz), extending west to the eastern edge of the Great Plains (Minnesota, Iowa, Kansas, Missouri). **Only the northernmost populations are migratory**; **birds south of approximately 40°N are largely resident year-round**. The winter range extends from southern Wisconsin, Oklahoma, Ohio, and southern New England southward to the Gulf Coast, and occasionally to Jalisco, the state of México, and Veracruz.

The western population (_B. l. elegans_) breeds from northern California to northern Baja California (approximately 26°N) west of the Sierra Nevada, and has recently expanded into Oregon west of the Cascades and into Arizona. This population is largely non-migratory, though regular local movements are reported in central California.

The species is a **partial migrant**. Only populations from the northern half of the eastern range undertake regular seasonal movements, traveling 300–1,500 km. Fall migration involves juveniles moving south from September through December and adults from October into December, peaking in late October to early November across New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Wisconsin. Spring migration peaks from late February through early April. Migrants follow leading lines along inland ridges and, more abundantly, along coastlines (Great Lakes and Atlantic coast), generally avoiding crossing large bodies of water. Flight speeds range from approximately 12–15 m/s, with faster speeds during inter-thermal glides (approximately 22 m/s). Migrants typically fly alone but may form small, loose flocks.

### California

In California, _**B. l. elegans**_**&#x20;is a year-round resident**. This population nests in **riparian and oak woodlands, eucalyptus groves, and suburban areas adjacent to forested regions throughout the state west of the Sierra Nevada. Suburban nesting is well documented** — active nests have been located as little as 2 m from buildings and 1 m from roads in southern California, and even large crowds playing sports directly under nest trees did not cause nest abandonment. In suburban southwestern Ohio (where the eastern _lineatus_ has also adapted to suburban life), breeding home ranges averaged 90 ha and contained an average of 169 houses each.

In California, calling becomes more regular and frequent between November and May, peaking January through April — the Red-shouldered Hawk is considered the **most vocal raptor in its California range**.

## Diet and Biology

The Red-shouldered Hawk is a **sit-and-wait predator** that hunts diurnally, primarily from perches within or at the edge of the forest canopy, dropping onto prey sighted below. Perch heights in Missouri ranged from 2 to 4 m above ground. In open habitats, birds may fly low and directly at prey in a harrier-like fashion, and ground hunting — catching small mammals as they emerge from burrows — has also been documented.&#x20;

The diet is broad and geographically variable. In northern and midwestern populations, **small mammals predominate** — especially meadow voles (_Microtus pennsylvanicus_), eastern chipmunks (_Tamias striatus_), and _Peromyscus_ mice. Amphibians (frogs, toads) and snakes become increasingly important in the southern portion of the range, and crayfish are a significant food item in some midwestern and southern populations. Birds (including mourning doves, house sparrows, and starlings — often taken at winter feeders in Wisconsin), insects (grasshoppers during outbreaks), and carrion are consumed opportunistically. Dietary flexibility is a hallmark of the species: individual populations switch prey emphasis from year to year in response to availability. In Iowa, birds shifted from predominantly amphibians in one year to mammals the next; in Arkansas, a similar interannual switch from amphibians to crayfish was documented. Pellet analyses are particularly unreliable for this species, as highly digestible amphibians and invertebrates are usually underrepresented.

The species is **strongly territorial and does not form flocks**. Home ranges typically range from 90–200 ha, with male ranges often larger than females' during the breeding season. Nest density varies widely, from 0.13 nests/km² in northern Wisconsin to 2.7 nests/km² in northeastern Arkansas. Both intra- and interspecific intruders near nests are chased and may be attacked — including Red-tailed Hawks, Great Horned Owls, Barred Owls, Golden Eagles, and even humans. .

Migrants arrive on territory from mid-February to mid-March depending on latitude; California residents begin pairing in February. Courtship includes the male's "sky-dance" — soaring upward while calling, followed by a series of steep dives, each checked by wide spirals and rapid ascents — culminating in a rapid dive onto the perched female's back for copulation (lasting 2–12 seconds). Courtship lasts approximately three weeks. Both sexes build or refurbish the nest, which is placed below the canopy but more than halfway up a tree, generally in a crotch of the main trunk, and often near water. Nest height ranges from approximately 12 to 19 m. Pairs frequently reuse nests across years (up to six consecutive years documented). A single clutch of 2–5 eggs (typically 3–4 in eastern populations, smaller in Florida and California) is laid at 2–3 day intervals. Eggs are dull white or faintly bluish with variable brown and lavender blotches. Incubation begins before clutch completion, resulting in asynchronous hatching (up to 5–7 days between first and last egg). Incubation lasts approximately 33 days per egg. Both sexes incubate, though the female performs the majority and usually spends nights on the nest. The male provides nearly all food to the female and nestlings until late in the nestling period. Nestlings fledge at approximately 35–45 days and begin successful hunting by 7–8 weeks after fledging, initially catching mostly insects and progressively capturing vertebrate prey by 10–13 weeks. Total dependence on parents lasts approximately 17–19 weeks. Most birds do not breed until at least one year of age, though yearling females occasionally breed with adult males (approximately 5–10% of pairs).

Great Horned Owls and raccoons are the most significant predators, destroying nesting adults, eggs, and young. Great Horned Owls and Red-tailed Hawks may usurp Red-shouldered Hawk nests.

The species is **highly vocal**, particularly during the early breeding season. The most common call is the _kee-aah_ — a 2–3 second call with accent on the first syllable and a drawn-out, downward-inflected second syllable, typically performed 5–12 times in sequence. This call is frequently mimicked by Blue Jays (_Cyanocitta cristata_). Seven distinct call types are recognized. The Red-shouldered Hawk is considered the diurnal ecological counterpart of the Barred Owl — both species occupy overlapping ranges, prefer the same moist woodland habitats, and exploit similar prey bases.

## Molt

The Red-shouldered Hawk follows a **Simple Basic Strategy** with a **Definitive Prebasic Molt that is complete — all feathers are replaced within a single annual cycle. This is in contrast to larger buteos** such as the Red-tailed Hawk and Swainson's Hawk, which undergo incomplete prebasic molts and exhibit Staffelmauser (stepwise molt with multiple simultaneous wave fronts).

**Prejuvenile (First Prebasic) Molt** is complete and occurs in the nest. Primaries begin to emerge at approximately 2 weeks of age, followed within a few days by secondaries, tertiaries, and rectrices. Body plumage appears progressively: wing and tail coverts first, then scapulars and breast, progressing anteriorly to the head and posteriorly to the rump. The last down is retained on the head, center of breast, and thighs. Rectrices appear at about 15 days. By time of nest departure at approximately 5 weeks, young still show some down on the head and wing coverts, and tail feathers are not completely grown.

**Definitive Prebasic Molt** is complete, beginning as early as April and lasting until October. The order of feather replacement has not been formally described. Because the molt is complete within a single annual cycle, this species does not exhibit the Staffelmauser replacement patterns seen in larger raptors. No preformative or prealternate molt has been documented.

Juvenile plumage is worn for approximately 18 months. The transition to definitive adult plumage — including the rufous barring on the underparts, the black-and-white tail banding, the white wing crescent, and the rufous shoulder patches — occurs with the first complete prebasic molt at approximately one year of age.
