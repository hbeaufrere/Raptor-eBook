# Swainson's Hawk (Buteo swainsoni)

## Description

### General Appearance

Swainson's Hawk is a medium-to-large buteo with a distinctly slimmer silhouette than most of its congeners — **narrower wings, a thinner body, and a more tapered, pointed wingtip profile**. In soaring flight, the wings are held in a **pronounced dihedral**, reminiscent of a Turkey Vulture, and the contrast between **dark flight feathers and paler wing linings (in light morphs)** is one of the most reliable field marks. The tail is grayish to grayish-brown with multiple narrow dark bands, a wide subterminal band, and a white to buffy tip in fresh plumage. White barring on the uppertail coverts creates a pale U at the base of the tail, particularly visible in flight. Perched birds show long wings that reach or just barely exceed the tail tip.

The species is markedly **polymorphic**, with plumage varying nearly continuously from **light to dark and including a rufous (intermediate) morph**. Three broad morph categories are recognized for convenience, though the variation is essentially clinal.

### Adult Plumage

**Light morph** adults are the most frequently encountered form. The head is slaty gray with a **white chin** and whitish forehead. **The defining feature is a solid rufous breast band — the "bib"** — forming a sharp boundary between the pale lower breast and belly and the darker throat and upper chest. The dorsal surface is a uniform muted grayish-brown. In flight, **white underwing coverts contrast sharply with darker remiges**; a **blackish carpal patch** is formed by the tips of the outermost underprimary coverts.

**Rufous (intermediate) morph** adults show a similar head pattern but have a dark gray or brown breast band, with the lower breast and belly solid rufous, variably streaked or barred.

**Dark morph** adults are nearly uniformly blackish-brown on the body, with white markings on the face reduced or absent. The remiges are similar to those of light morphs but darker overall. Undertail coverts remain buffy white with dark barring — a consistent distinguishing feature from all other dark-morph North American buteos.

### Juvenile Plumage

Juveniles share the same underwing pattern as adults of the corresponding morph. Light morph juveniles have creamy to ochraceous underparts **heavily streaked with dark brown**, most densely on the sides of the upper breast; streaking extends to the belly. The head shows a pale supercilium and cheeks, a dark malar mark, and a dark-streaked crown. The back is mottled. The undertail coverts remain primarily whitish in all juvenile morphs. Later subadult plumages often show a much paler — sometimes nearly white — head.

### Differences Between Sexes

**Females are substantially larger than males**, though the sexes are otherwise similar in plumage pattern. **Females more frequently exhibit dark morph coloration**; in northeastern California approximately 70% of females and 40% of males are dark. Females also tend to have more distinct barring and a wider dark subterminal band in the rectrices. Body mass provides the clearest sex distinction: adult males in Alberta average 808 g versus 1,109 g in females. Forearm length, wing chord, and tail length have been used as predictors of sex in wintering birds.

### Bare Parts

The bill is slaty to blackish, with a pale olive buff corner of the mouth. The cere and mouth lining are pale greenish-yellow to yellow. Leg and foot color varies from wax yellow to creamy or light grayish-green, and is generally paler in immatures than adults. Iris color is age-dependent: gray or blue-gray in juveniles, becoming yellowish in the first year, pale brown in the second, and dark brownish to reddish-brown in adults.

### Subspecies

Buteo swainsoni is **monotypic**. No subspecies are currently recognized. The absence of geographic variation is consistent with genetic data showing no population structure across the breeding range.

## Distribution and Migration

### Overall Range

Swainson's Hawk breeds across the **open grasslands, shrublands, and agricultural areas of western North America**, from prairie Canada south through the Great Plains and intermountain west to northern Mexico. The breeding range extends north to portions of Washington and Oregon east of the Cascades, southern Idaho, western Montana, southern Alberta, central Saskatchewan, and southwestern Manitoba. The eastern boundary runs through western Minnesota, central Nebraska and Kansas, central Oklahoma, and central Texas. **A disjunct population breeds in the Sacramento and San Joaquin valleys of California and in adjacent Sierra Nevada foothill valleys**.

Swainson's Hawk is a complete **long-distance migrant**. **Nearly the entire breeding population migrates annually to wintering grounds on the pampas of Argentina** — primarily the provinces of Córdoba, Santa Fe, Buenos Aires, Entre Ríos, and La Pampa — with smaller numbers in adjacent Uruguay. Round-trip migration distances can exceed 20,000 km, making it the **longest-distance migrant among North American buteos**, second among all raptors only to the Arctic Peregrine Falcon.

Migration is diurnal, relying on thermal updrafts, and typically proceeds in large, **often spectacular flocks (also called kettles)**. The species concentrates dramatically through the narrow corridor of Central America: up to 845,000 individuals have been counted in a single autumn in Veracruz, Mexico, and nearly 350,000 at a single point in Panama City. Birds converge on the Gulf coast of eastern Mexico, funnel through Central America along the Pacific slope, cross the Andes in Colombia, and travel along the eastern Andean foothills into central Argentina.

Fall migration begins as flocks gather in late August and early September. Most individuals depart breeding areas in Canada by late August to mid-September and reach Argentina by early November, averaging approximately 190 km per day.

Spring migration begins in late February to mid-March in Argentina, with birds returning to Canadian breeding grounds between late April and early May, averaging approximately 150 km per day over roughly 60 days.

### California — General

California represents an important but substantially reduced portion of the species' historical range. Swainson's Hawk is **listed as Threatened in California, where the breeding population has declined by an estimated 90% from historical levels** — a decline corroborated by both museum egg collections and microsatellite genetic analyses, the latter suggesting the contraction began approximately 200 years ago with European settlement. The current California breeding population is estimated at 1,800–2,000 nesting pairs, compared to an estimated pre-European population of approximately 20,000 nesting pairs.

Encouragingly, satellite survey data indicate the California summering population grew at a rate of approximately 13.9% per year between 2005 and 2018, consistent with broader raptor population rebounds in North America. However, the species remains **largely extirpated from southern California**, where it was historically common, and the ongoing expansion of orchards and vineyards — crop types unsuitable for foraging — limits the significance of this recovery.

The **largest concentrations now occur in Yolo, Solano, Sacramento, and San Joaquin counties**. Nests are typically placed at the edges of narrow riparian strips, in isolated oak woodland, and in lone or roadside trees within an agricultural matrix. The species relies heavily on alfalfa fields and other irrigated hay crops for foraging, with home ranges averaging 40–87 km² in the Central Valley; hawks have been documented traveling up to 10 km from the nest to forage. Orchards and vineyards are rarely used.

### The Central Valley Population — A Distinct Migratory Ecotype

The Central Valley (CV) population of Swainson's Hawk is remarkable in representing a behaviorally and ecologically distinct migratory ecotype within the species. Satellite telemetry studies have revealed that CV birds diverge fundamentally from the rest of the North American population **in both their migratory route and their wintering destinations**.

While most Swainson's Hawks migrate all the way to the pampas region of Argentina, the Central Valley population is unique: **the majority of birds migrate only as far south as Mexico, although some continue to Central and South America**. The Central Valley birds primarily winter in western Mexico and Central America, although some reach Colombia and northern Argentina. By contrast, every Swainson's Hawk that nests outside the Valley and has been tracked migrated to Argentina.

**Central Valley birds substantially use stopover areas during southward migration** — averaging 53 days per individual at stopovers — but return more directly from wintering to breeding areas, with an average of only 0.3 stopover days per individual during spring. Most stopovers occur in western Mexico and the San Joaquin Valley itself. Land cover in stopover areas is dominated by agriculture and shrub-scrub.

Duration of the CV hawks' southward migration averages approximately 99 days per individual — longer than for Great Basin–Great Plains birds (averaging 66 days) — despite a substantially shorter migration distance, apparently because **CV hawks spend more time in stopover areas**.

In terms of phenology, CV hawks arrive in the Central Valley in late February and early March, 4–6 weeks earlier than hawks at sites less than 350 km away in northeastern California, a pattern likely related to their shorter migration distance to wintering sites in Mexico. After the young have left the nest, Swainson's Hawks gather in groups and leave California by mid-October to fly south.

The diversity of wintering areas used by the CV population may provide resilience from the effects of human activities compared to collective wintering in a single area — an advantage over the rest of the species, which concentrates in the Argentine pampas and was devastated by monocrotophos poisoning in the 1990s. Advantages of using northern wintering areas also include less time spent in migration and earlier arrival on breeding areas.

Research shows that CV Swainson's Hawks do not breed with the Great Basin or Great Plains population, nor do they winter in the same locations. Genetic analyses suggest the California population may be distinct from the remainder of the breeding population in North America, consistent with the markedly different migratory strategy.

## Diet and Biology

### Diet

Swainson's Hawk shows a **remarkable seasonal dietary switch unparalleled among North American buteos**. During the breeding season, it functions as a typical open-country predator of vertebrates; outside the breeding season, it is almost exclusively insectivorous.

**Breeding season: The diet is dominated by small mammals** — primarily Richardson's ground squirrels in the Canadian prairie, pocket gophers and voles in the Pacific states, and rabbits in Utah and New Mexico. Reptiles and birds contribute regionally; in Washington, the diet includes approximately 20% reptiles and 19% birds. Insects, while numerically abundant in stomachs, contribute little on a biomass basis during the nesting period.

**Non-breeding season: Insects — particularly grasshoppers** (Acrididae, principally Dichroplus spp. in Argentina), dragonflies, lepidopteran larvae, and beetles — dominate the diet overwhelmingly. Invertebrates comprise up to 94% of food items in non-breeding North American birds by count. A single individual may consume an average of 100 grasshoppers per day. In Argentina, flocks of up to 12,000 hawks forage almost exclusively on insects in alfalfa fields and grasslands. Only by concentrating on such abundant prey can the massive non-breeding aggregations of this species be sustained.

### Foraging Behavior

During the breeding season, Swainson's Hawk hunts by soaring over open country, perch-hunting from trees, poles, and elevated ground, or walking and running on the ground in pursuit of prey. It is well known for following farm equipment to take advantage of rodents flushed by cultivation, and for congregating around field burning and flood irrigation events. Groups perch near ground squirrel burrows waiting for prey to emerge, and individuals walk and run after grasshoppers in the manner of domestic turkeys. Aerial insect capture — snatching prey in flight and consuming it on the wing — is also common and represents the most successful foraging mode when insects are present (65% capture success versus 42% on the ground in Argentina). Breeding pairs defend territories aggressively near the nest but forage communally in nearby fields, particularly during harvest.

### Reproduction

Pair formation begins on arrival on breeding grounds, from early March in California to late April in Canada. Courtship includes dramatic aerial sky-dance displays — steep dives and recoveries from high circling flight — performed with decreasing intensity as the season progresses. Pairs are typically monogamous and frequently return to the same mate and nest site across multiple years (mean pair duration 5 years in central California; individuals up to 24 years old have been documented returning to breeding sites). Cooperative breeding (polyandry and helper males) has been observed occasionally in northeastern California.

Nest construction begins 7–15 days after arrival. Nests are bulky stick structures, typically near the top of isolated trees along stream courses, in shelterbelts, or in agricultural settings. A single clutch of 1–4 eggs is laid at approximately 2-day intervals. Incubation lasts 34–35 days, performed primarily by the female. Nestlings fledge at 38–46 days of age. Most individuals do not breed until 3 years of age.

## Molt

Swainson's Hawk follows a **Complex Basic Strategy**, comprising incomplete-to-complete prebasic molts and a limited (occasionally absent) preformative molt, with no prealternate molt.

Prejuvenile (First Prebasic) molt is complete and occurs in the nest from May to July, beginning at 9–10 days of age. By 34 days, contour and flight feathers are fully developed.

Preformative molt is limited to absent, occurring primarily on non-breeding grounds from September to March. Up to 40% of body feathers may be replaced, but no wing coverts or flight feathers are involved. This molt is absent in some individuals.

Second Prebasic molt is incomplete to complete (February to January) and may begin on wintering grounds in South America, suspend during migration, and complete on the breeding grounds or again during southbound migration. Outer juvenile primaries (among p6–p10), corresponding primary coverts, certain secondaries (among s3–s4 and s6–s10), and some rectrices are typically retained, initiating the Staffelmauser pattern. A complete molt occurs in approximately 15–20% of individuals at this stage.

Definitive Prebasic molt is incomplete to complete (May to March). It may begin during incubation, suspend during chick-rearing, and complete on non-breeding grounds. Primaries are replaced distally (p1→p10); secondaries are replaced proximally from s1 and s5 and distally from the tertials.

A key feature of adult wing molt is Staffelmauser (stepwise molt), in which incomplete prebasic molts leave multiple replacement wave fronts active simultaneously within the primaries and secondaries. This produces 2–4 generations of feathers within the wing after each molt cycle, allowing multiple small gaps rather than large single gaps, thereby preserving aerodynamic integrity during the demanding annual migration. Definitive plumage is typically attained following the Third Prebasic molt, though a small proportion of individuals may acquire it as early as the Second Prebasic molt.
