# Red-tailed hawk (Buteo jamaicensis)

## Description

The Red-tailed Hawk is a large, broad-winged buteo and one of the most common raptors across North America. Adults typically have a rich rufous-red tail (in the eastern _B. j. borealis_ and western _B. j. calurus_ subspecies), a **dark patagial bar on the leading edge of the underwing, and a streaked belly band across the otherwise white chest**. When looking from behind, a **speckled V on the back** is typically visible when perched. However, this species is highly variable, with up to **16 recognized subspecies** and considerable **polymorphism in western populations**. The **western calurus subspecies** ranges from light to dark morphs, with dark morphs being entirely dark brown to nearly black. The red tail of the calurus is more heavily barred than the borealis. Total length is approximately 45–65 cm with a wingspan of 110–145 cm. **Juveniles lack the red tail and instead have brown tails with multiple narrow dark bands**. The Harlan subspecies also does not have a red tail. Sexes are similar in plumage but females are notably larger (reversed sexual size dimorphism), averaging approximately 1,224 g compared to males at approximately 1,028 g.

**Sexes are similar in all plumage aspects** (no sexual dichromatism). **Females are notably larger than males** (reversed sexual size dimorphism), weighing approximately 1,224 g versus 1,028 g for males (for borealis, calurus can be slightly bigger). **Female wing chord is consistently longer than in males** across the range. Body mass combined with measurements is the most reliable method for sex determination; DNA sexing via blood sample or feather pulp is the most definitive approach in a clinical setting. Juveniles are best distinguished from adults by their tail: j**uvenile tails are brown with multiple narrow dark bands and lack the characteristic red coloration, whereas adult tails are rufous-red with a narrow dark subterminal band (and more bands in calurus)**. Juveniles have uniformly worn flight feathers (all from the same generation), while adults show mixed generations of primaries and secondaries in a Staffelmauser pattern. **The iris progresses from dark in hatchlings to grayish, then buff-yellow in juveniles, spectrum yellow during the first winter, and slowly grades to hair brown in older birds. Definitive adult plumage is typically attained with the third prebasic molt**.

Several subspecies may be encountered in California and the western US, and they differ appreciably in plumage. _**B. j. calurus**_**&#x20;(Western Red-tailed Hawk) is the predominant subspecies in California and is strongly polymorphic:** light morphs have a white chest with a dark belly band and rufous-red adult tail; rufous (intermediate) morphs show heavier rufous-brown streaking on the chest and flanks; and dark morphs are uniformly dark chocolate-brown to blackish with a rufous-red tail. _B. j. borealis_ (Eastern Red-tailed Hawk) occurs only as a light morph, with a clean white breast, well-defined belly band, and bright rufous tail in adults. _B. j. harlani_ (Harlan's Hawk) is the most distinct: adults are typically very dark overall with a variably mottled gray, white, or reddish tail lacking the typical rufous coloration; some individuals show a nearly white tail with dark mottling. _B. j. kriderii_ (Krider's Red-tailed Hawk) is the palest form, with a mostly white head, reduced or absent belly band, and a pale pinkish to whitish tail with a faint subterminal band. These subspecies intergrade where their ranges overlap, and intermediate individuals are common, making identification challenging in practice.

## Distribution and Migration

The Red-tailed Hawk breeds throughout North America from southern and central Alaska south to Panama, the Caribbean, and occasionally as far south as Venezuela. It is found in a great variety of habitats but **typically associated with open areas interspersed with woodland**, and is generally absent from large expanses of treeless terrain or dense unbroken forest. The global population is estimated at more than 2,000,000 individuals, with roughly 90% residing in North America. Sixteen subspecies are recognized, divided into two groups: Harlan's Hawk (B. j. harlani) and the "typical" Red-tailed Hawks. Migration is diurnal and varies with latitude. Northern populations are mostly migratory, with movements typically under 1,500 km. Southern breeding populations in the United States and northern Mexico are largely year-round residents.

**In California, the Red-tailed Hawk is common to abundant year-round throughout the state**. California supports some of the highest breeding densities reported anywhere in the range. The species has expanded its range historically through California in response to forest clearing for agriculture and urban development. This is the most commonly admitted raptor species at most wildlife rehabilitation centers in California.

## Diet and Biology

The Red-tailed Hawk is an **opportunistic predator with a broad diet of small to medium-sized vertebrates**. Primary prey includes rodents (voles, mice, ground squirrels), rabbits and hares, snakes, and birds. **In California, spring diet composition is approximately 61% ground squirrels (Otospermophilus beecheyi), 13% pocket gophers (Thomomys bottae), and 10% cottontails (Sylvilagus audubonii)**. The species is primarily a sit-and-wait predator, with 60-80% of hunting done from elevated perches. It also hunts by soaring, hovering, and occasionally on foot. Fresh carrion is readily consumed, especially in winter. A caged adult consumes an average of 140 g/day (approximately 17% of body weight); daily intake is higher in winter than in summer.

The Red-tailed Hawk is generally **monogamous**, with sedentary pairs remaining together year-round. Territories are vigorously defended, typically ranging from 1.25 to over 2.5 km2. Nests are bulky stick structures placed in tall trees, on cliff ledges, or on man-made structures; the same nest may be reused over multiple years. Clutch size is typically 2-3 eggs (range 1-4). Incubation lasts approximately 28 days, shared by both parents. Young are altricial, fledging at approximately 42-46 days. Fledglings remain near the nest for 2-3 weeks post-fledging, develop sustained flight within approximately 2.5 weeks, and may remain associated with parents for up to 10 weeks in migratory populations and up to 6 months in resident populations. One brood per season, but pairs will renest if eggs are lost. The species is long-lived, with individuals reaching 25+ years in the wild and 29.5+ years in captivity.

## Molting

The Red-tailed Hawk exhibits a **Modified Basic Strategy** with complete prebasic molts and a limited preformative molt in some individuals, but no prealternate molts. The species has 10 functional primaries, 14-16 secondaries, and 12 rectrices. Primaries are replaced distally (p1 to p10), secondaries from three foci (proximally from s1 and s5 and distally from the tertials), and rectrices in the sequence r1-r6-r3-r4-r2-r5 on each side. Due to the large flight feather complement, definitive prebasic molt is incomplete and protracted (April-November), exhibiting a Staffelmauser pattern where 2-4 waves of replacement proceed simultaneously through the wing. This stepwise strategy maintains flight integrity by producing only small gaps at any given time. The second prebasic molt (second Summer) occurs from approximately February to October and is typically incomplete, retaining 1-4 outer juvenile primaries, 1-6 juvenile secondaries, and 1-4 juvenile rectrices. In a clinical context, molt status is highly useful for aging birds and understanding their feather condition for rehabilitation planning.
