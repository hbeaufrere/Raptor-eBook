# Bald Eagle (_Haliaeetus leucocephalus_)

---

## Description

### General Appearance

The Bald Eagle is a large fish-eagle — one of the largest raptors in North America — with a massive body, broad, plank-like wings held flat in soaring flight, a large head and bill, and a relatively short, slightly wedge-shaped tail. Total length is 71–96 cm, wingspan 168–244 cm, and body mass 3.0–6.3 kg, with the largest individuals in northern populations (Alaska, Canada) and the smallest in the southeastern and southwestern United States. In flight, the head and neck project forward approximately half the length of the tail, and the wingbeats are slow, powerful, and methodical. The species soars and glides on broad wings held largely flat and at a right angle to the body — distinguishing it from the Golden Eagle, which holds its wings slightly above horizontal.

The Bald Eagle is one of the most recognizable raptors in the world owing to its adult plumage: a uniformly dark brown body contrasting starkly with a pure white head, tail, and tail coverts. However, this definitive adult appearance is not acquired until 4.5–5.5 years of age, after a prolonged and complex plumage maturation sequence that makes aging subadult birds challenging. Approximately 25% of individuals attain full adult plumage by 4.5 years; most or all do so by 5.5 years.

### Adult Plumage

In Definitive Basic Plumage, the head, neck, tail, uppertail coverts, and undertail coverts are white, sometimes with faint gray flecking around the eye. The remainder of the body plumage is uniformly dark brown, with contour feathers occasionally showing buff margins from wear. The bill, cere, iris, legs, and feet are bright yellow — a striking combination with the white head that makes the adult unmistakable among North American raptors. The remiges show 2–4 sets of basic feathers in Staffelmauser patterns, with replacement sets identifiable by the juxtaposition of worn and fresh feathers. Some adults retain heavy dark flecking around the eyes through 8.5 years of age, possibly reflecting slow maturation or atypical molt timing.

### Juvenile Plumage

Juveniles are generally dark brown throughout, with variable white or pale mottling on the underwing coverts, axillaries, and posterior underparts. Body feathers of the upperparts are dark with white bases that create a mottled appearance when ruffled. The tail is mottled white, gray, and dark brown, often with a poorly defined dark terminal band contrasting with paler middle portions. The chin is buffy brown to smoke gray, sometimes extending to the throat; the belly is buffy brown (tawny) with variable dark mottling, typically contrasting with the darker breast. The bill and cere are blackish gray, and the iris is dark brown. Juvenile primaries, secondaries, and median wing coverts are longer and more tapered than in later plumages, and all secondaries are equal in length — a useful aging criterion, as later plumages show secondaries of differing lengths reflecting mixed feather generations.

The progression from juvenile to adult plumage spans approximately four to five years and passes through several recognizable intermediate stages. In Second Basic Plumage (approximately 1.5–2.5 years), the crown becomes noticeably paler, a whitish triangular patch typically appears on the mantle, white mottling increases on the underwing coverts, and the belly becomes variably whiter. In Third Basic Plumage (approximately 2.5–3.5 years), the head and nape become variably smoke gray to whitish with dark brown auriculars that create the appearance of a broad dark eye-stripe, and the throat becomes white to buffy. By Fourth and Fifth Basic Plumages (approximately 3.5–5.5 years), the head is mostly white with variable residual brown flecking, particularly around the eyes, and the tail is predominantly white with limited brown mottling at the proximal feather edges. The bill and cere progressively lighten from blackish gray in juveniles through dull yellow to bright yellow in adults. Iris color transitions from dark brown in juveniles to buffy brown (second cycle), pale yellow (third cycle), and bright yellow in adults. These progressive bare-part color changes can assist with aging birds when plumage characteristics are ambiguous.

### Differences Between Sexes

The female is approximately 25% larger and heavier than the male, consistent with the reverse sexual dimorphism characteristic of raptors. In Alaska, adult females averaged 5.35 kg (range 4.64–6.41 kg) and adult males 4.23 kg (range 3.68–4.86 kg). Size and mass overlap exists between northern males and southern females, making sex determination of birds of unknown geographic origin inadvisable based on morphometrics alone. Multivariate analyses of morphometric measurements can discriminate the sexes with greater than 98% accuracy when geographic origin is known. The sexes are identical in plumage at all ages. Females reportedly give a unique soft, high-pitched call during copulation that is not given by males.

### Bare Parts

In hatchlings, the bill is blackish gray and the cere is pale gray, transitioning through pale yellow, pale olive, and medium gray during the first two weeks. In juveniles, the bill and cere are blackish gray to dusky. During the second cycle, the bill begins grading to dull yellow near the cere, and the cere becomes light gray. By the third cycle, the bill is predominantly yellow, often retaining a dark line along the dorsal ridge extending from the nares, and the cere is mostly yellow with dark mottling. In adults, the bill and cere are uniformly bright yellow. The iris transitions from dark brown in hatchlings and juveniles, to buffy brown or cream during the second cycle, to dull or pale yellow during the third cycle, and to bright yellow in adults. Legs and feet are yellow in juveniles and adults; talons are black at all ages.

### Subspecies

Two subspecies have traditionally been recognized based on body size, though the validity of this division is debated because size variation is clinal with latitude, movement among populations may span thousands of kilometers, and no clear morphological demarcation exists between northern and southern populations. Several authorities recognize only a single, monotypic species.

**H. l. washingtoniensis** — The northern form. Breeds from the Aleutian Islands and Alaska across Canada east to Labrador and Nova Scotia, south to Oregon, Utah, central Kansas, and Illinois. Larger than the southern form, but size variation is clinal. No plumage differences from the nominate subspecies.

**H. l. leucocephalus** — The nominate and southern form. Breeds in the southern United States from California and Arizona east to Virginia and Florida, south into northwestern Mexico (Baja California and Sonora). Smaller than the northern form, but again, size variation is clinal with no discrete boundary.

The taxonomic history of the northern subspecies name is notably convoluted. The name _washingtoniensis_ traces to Audubon's "Bird of Washington," a specimen whose existence and identity have been questioned — described by one historian as "an elaborate lie." Despite this, the name has priority and is used when subspecific division is recognized.

## Distribution and Migration

### Overall Range

The Bald Eagle breeds near aquatic ecosystems — rivers, lakes, reservoirs, and coastal bays — with forested shorelines or cliffs throughout much of North America. The breeding range extends from the southern Brooks Range in Alaska across Canada to Labrador and Newfoundland, and through every state in the contiguous United States. Large breeding concentrations are found along the entire Atlantic Coast, in the Great Lakes region (Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota), in the Pacific Northwest (northern California through western Washington), in southeastern Alaska, and in Florida. Very small numbers breed in Mexico at Magdalena Bay (Baja California Sur), southern Sonora, and Chihuahua, where populations remain perilously close to extirpation.

The most recent population estimate for the contiguous United States (2018–2019 data) is approximately 316,700 individuals and 71,400 occupied nesting territories — a greater than 400% increase from the 2009 estimate and a dramatic recovery from a nadir of approximately 417 breeding pairs in 1963. An estimated 58,000 adults occupy the northern Pacific Coast from southern British Columbia to the Alaska Peninsula, where populations appear to have stabilized near carrying capacity since 1990–2000.

The migration strategy is complex and variable, differing by age, breeding latitude, coastal versus interior location, winter climate, and food availability. Northern interior populations (breeding north of approximately 40°N) generally migrate south from August through January, with immatures departing first and migrating farther south. Southern adults (breeding south of 40°N) are typically year-round residents. In the Pacific Northwest and Alaska, migratory movements are closely linked to salmon runs, with birds moving north along the coast to access early runs (e.g., the Chilkat River, Alaska, where thousands of eagles congregate in fall) before following the runs south. Spring migration is more directed than fall migration; northern breeders return to breeding grounds from January to March as soon as weather and food availability permit.

The Bald Eagle generally migrates alone, using thermals and orographic lift, and does not form the large kettles characteristic of buteos. However, large congregations occur at exceptional foraging and roosting sites. Migration speeds average 50 km/h over entire routes, with peaks up to 144 km/h; daily distances range from 33 km/d (juveniles) to over 200 km/d (adults). Adults migrating northward in spring may not forage en route, losing up to 25% of body mass over approximately 2,000 km.

The species was listed as Endangered in the contiguous United States in 1978 under the Endangered Species Act, downlisted to Threatened in 1995, and delisted in 2007 — one of the most celebrated conservation success stories in North American history.

### California

California's Bald Eagle breeding population was devastated by DDT. By the late 1960s and early 1970s, the species no longer nested in central California, and the remaining breeding population was confined to the interior mountains of the northern one-third of the state, consisting of only 26 pairs concentrated in eight forested northern California counties. All nests at that time were in ponderosa pine and mixed-conifer forests at higher elevations.

Following the ban of DDT in 1972 and federal listing, recovery began slowly. By 1985, intensified survey efforts detected 65 known territories in nine counties, but virtually all remained in montane conifer habitats. The Pacific Bald Eagle Recovery Plan set a target of 800 breeding pairs across the western United States and did not anticipate colonization of the Central Valley or Sierra Nevada foothills.

The population has since exceeded these recovery targets. Recent research documents range expansion and population increase in the Sierra Nevada foothills and Central Valley over 2011–2024, with eagles colonizing low-elevation habitats along foothill reservoirs and rivers — areas where they were not expected to nest. Nest trees in this newly colonized central interior region include gray pines, Fremont cottonwoods, and western sycamores, a marked departure from the exclusively montane conifer nesting of the 1970s and 1980s.

The breeding season in California extends primarily from February through July. Wintering Bald Eagles are widespread in the state, concentrating at lakes, reservoirs, and river systems, including significant numbers in the Klamath Basin (shared with Oregon), Sacramento Valley, and San Joaquin Valley. California juveniles tracked by satellite dispersed northward after fledging — in some cases reaching British Columbia and the Northwest Territories — before returning to California during fall and winter.

In southern California, the species was extirpated as a breeder by 1960. Reintroduction efforts on Santa Catalina Island beginning in the 1980s have established a small resident population of approximately 15–20 eagles including five breeding pairs; however, persistent DDE contamination from historical ocean dumping off the Palos Verdes Peninsula continues to impair reproductive success, requiring ongoing egg manipulation and chick fostering.

The Bald Eagle remains listed as Endangered by the State of California, even though it was federally delisted in 2007. Ongoing threats in the state include habitat loss, human disturbance, collisions with vehicles and wind turbines, electrocution, lead and rodenticide exposure, and emerging infectious diseases including highly pathogenic avian influenza (H5N1).

## Diet and Biology

The Bald Eagle is an opportunistic forager whose diet varies considerably across its range based on local food availability. It obtains food through three primary strategies: direct capture of live prey, scavenging of carrion, and kleptoparasitism (stealing food from other species or from conspecifics). Fish are preferred whenever available; combined data from 20 studies across the species' range found the diet of nesting Bald Eagles to be approximately 56% fish, 28% birds, 14% mammals, and 2% other items. Fish species most frequently taken live are those occurring near the water surface, including gizzard shad, threadfin shad, and white bass. Benthic-dwelling species such as catfish are most often scavenged when found dead or dying at the surface. In the Pacific Northwest and Alaska, post-spawning salmon carcasses are the dominant food resource when available. Avian prey includes waterfowl (especially Canada Goose and Mallard), American Coot, gulls, cormorants, and Great Blue Heron. Large mammals (primarily cervids) are almost exclusively consumed as carrion, especially in winter, while small to medium-sized mammals — hares, rabbits, muskrat, ground squirrels — may be taken alive.

The species hunts primarily from perches or on the wing, typically within 500 m of shoreline. It soars to locate prey, then stoops to capture it with one or both feet. Repeated stoops are often required when hunting waterfowl on water, and success rates are often poor. In one Nebraska study, hunting eagles achieved a 24% success rate across nearly 2,000 foraging searches, with 73% of actual strikes successful. Kleptoparasitism is well documented, with Osprey being the most frequent victim. Adults are more proficient foragers than immatures owing to superior experience and skill; immatures rely more heavily on scavenging and soaring to locate carrion.

The Bald Eagle is monogamous and thought to mate for life. Courtship includes spectacular aerial displays: the Cartwheel Display (paired birds lock talons at altitude and tumble earthward, separating near the ground), the Chase Display, and the Roller-Coaster Flight. Copulation is seldom observed in the wild and typically lasts 5–15 seconds. The pair bond may persist year-round in nonmigratory populations; in migratory populations, pairs may winter together and maintain the bond across years.

Nest-building begins one to three months prior to egg-laying. Nests are massive stick structures placed in the uppermost quarter of large, accessible trees (typically super-canopy trees near water), or on cliffs and ground sites in treeless regions. Both sexes contribute to construction. Nests are reused across years and may achieve enormous dimensions — the largest on record, in St. Petersburg, Florida, was 2.9 m in diameter and 6.1 m tall; a famous Ohio nest, used for 34 years, weighed an estimated 2 metric tons. Pairs average 1.5 nests per territory and may switch nest sites in successive years, especially after failures.

Clutch size is typically 1–3 eggs (79% of nests contain 2 eggs), laid at daily or near-daily intervals over 3–6 days. Eggs are dull white and rough-shelled. Incubation begins after the first egg, resulting in asynchronous hatching (1–4 days between hatchlings). The incubation period is approximately 35 days. Both sexes incubate, with the female performing the majority (53–75% of daylight hours). Hatching asynchrony gives the first chick a significant competitive advantage, and siblicide — particularly starvation of the youngest nestling — is common when food is limited.

Nestlings are brooded intensely for the first 2–3 weeks (approximately 80–90% of daylight hours), predominantly by the female. Both sexes provision the young; the male provides most food during the first 2 weeks while the female tends the nest, after which both contribute roughly equally. Prey is delivered 2–5 times per day and nestlings are fed approximately 8 times daily during the first 2 weeks. Young begin self-feeding at approximately 6 weeks. Fledging occurs at 8–14 weeks of age, with males tending to fledge earlier than females regardless of hatching order (mean 78 vs. 82 days in Saskatchewan). Fledglings remain dependent on parental feeding for 4–10 weeks after nest departure, during which time they develop foraging skills through trial and error — initially scavenging fish carcasses before progressing to active hunting. Total dependence on parents lasts approximately 17–23 weeks. Most individuals do not breed until at least 5 years of age (after Definitive Plumage is attained), though breeding by younger birds in immature plumage has been documented, especially in expanding populations.

The Bald Eagle is strongly territorial during the breeding season, defending an area typically of 0.5–4 km² around the nest. Both sexes defend the territory through prominent perching, vocalizations, and chasing of intruders. Territorial encounters rarely involve physical contact but can be fatal when they do. Territory and mate fidelity is high; marked individuals have occupied the same territory for up to 29 years. Natal dispersal distance averages approximately 69 km, with females dispersing roughly twice as far as males (median 138 km vs. 60 km).

Despite its iconic status, the Bald Eagle's vocalizations are surprisingly weak and high-pitched — often described as a rapid, snickering series of piping notes (_kwit kwit kwit kee-kee-kee-kee-ker_) reminiscent of certain gull calls. The scream commonly attributed to the Bald Eagle in popular media is actually the call of the Red-tailed Hawk. Three primary call types are recognized: the Keek (a rapid descending series, the most common call), the Scream (a prolonged, gull-like cry), and the Bark (a low-pitched, short nasal note given near the nest).

Mortality is predominantly human-caused. Of nearly 3,000 Bald Eagles necropsied from 1982–2013 at the USGS National Wildlife Health Center, the leading causes of death were poisoning (25.6%, predominantly lead), trauma from collisions (22.9%), electrocution (12.5%), gunshot (10.2%), emaciation (5.9%), and disease (5.2%). Lead poisoning — primarily from ingestion of lead ammunition fragments in hunter-shot carcasses and waterfowl — remains the single most important toxicological threat. Highly pathogenic avian influenza (H5N1) caused documented mortalities in 29 states in 2022. Other diseases of note include aspergillosis, avian vacuolar myelinopathy (linked to a cyanobacterial toxin bioaccumulated through American Coot feeding on Hydrilla), avian pox, and West Nile virus. First-year survival averages approximately 69% (range 50–100% across populations); annual adult survival is generally high (80–100%). Wild longevity records include 38 years (New York) and 33 years (Wisconsin, Kansas).

## Molt

The Bald Eagle follows a **Modified Basic Strategy**, comprising incomplete prebasic molts and a limited preformative molt in some individuals, with no prealternate molt.

**Prejuvenile (First Prebasic) Molt** is the only complete molt. It occurs primarily from April into July in the nest. Second down emerges at days 9–11; flight feathers emerge at 2–3 weeks; body contour feathers begin with the humeral tract at 3–4 weeks. Feathering of the head and back appears at 4–5 weeks, lateral ventral surfaces at 4–6 weeks, and tarsi last at 6–8 weeks. Juvenile plumage is essentially complete at 11–14 weeks, usually after fledging, though outer primaries and rectrices may require 2 or more additional weeks.

**Preformative Molt** is limited, occurring primarily from December into April, and includes up to 40% of body feathers scattered over the head and body. No flight feathers or wing coverts are replaced. This molt is absent in some individuals and has only recently been recognized as a distinct inserted first-cycle molt separate from the Second Prebasic Molt.

**Second Prebasic Molt** is incomplete, occurring primarily from February into November. It includes most body feathers and upperwing coverts but retains 3–5 juvenile outer primaries, corresponding primary coverts, 1–5 or more juvenile secondaries (among s2–s4 and s7–s12), and up to 7 outer rectrices. This molt initiates the Staffelmauser (stepwise) replacement pattern.

**Third Prebasic Molt** is incomplete, primarily March to December. It continues where the Second Prebasic Molt arrested and may initiate new replacement waves at p1, s1, s5, and the tertials. By this molt, all juvenile body feathers and rectrices are replaced. Occasionally 1–2 juvenile outer primaries or secondaries persist.

**Definitive Prebasic Molt** is incomplete, occurring primarily from March into December (occasionally extending through February). Molt of flight feathers may commence during incubation, suspend for chick-rearing, and complete on wintering grounds. Replacement proceeds in 2–4 simultaneous waves through the wing in Staffelmauser patterns, producing 2–4 generations of feathers following each molt cycle. Rectrices at various positions may be retained, resulting in 2–3 feather generations. The Staffelmauser pattern preserves flight integrity by producing only small gaps in the wing at any time — critical for a species that depends on sustained soaring for foraging and migration.

Definitive adult plumage appearance is achieved by approximately 25% of individuals after the Fourth Prebasic Molt (age 4.5 years) and by most or all individuals after the Fifth Prebasic Molt (age 5.5 years), though the definitive molt strategy itself stabilizes by the Fourth Prebasic Molt.
