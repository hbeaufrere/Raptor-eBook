# West nile virus

West Nile virus (WNV) is a flavivirus transmitted primarily by Culex spp. mosquitoes; hippoboscid flies may also serve as mechanical vectors. In raptors, WNV can produce sudden death along with neurologic, ocular, cardiac, and dermatologic signs. Species susceptibility and clinical manifestations vary widely. Strigidae from northern regions appear particularly severely affected. Among accipitrids, the red-tailed hawk, Cooper's hawk, and sharp-shinned hawk have been identified as especially susceptible in retrospective analyses of free-ranging birds. Neurologic manifestations range from subtle abnormalities to severe ataxia, seizures, and vestibular dysfunction. Chronic infection may produce characteristic feather abnormalities, including a "pinched off" feather calamus or stented feathers, particularly in Falconiformes and Accipitriformes, though these changes have also been reported in eagles and owls. Chorioretinitis and chorioretinal scarring may be identified on ophthalmic examination.



Raptors typically develop overt clinical disease several weeks after initial exposure and are rarely viremic at the time of clinical presentation; consequently, blood-based PCR is usually negative. Viral shedding through saliva and the cloaca persists longer than viremia but also ceases before or shortly after clinical signs become apparent. Therefore, PCR testing of oropharyngeal or cloacal swabs is generally nondiagnostic. Tissue-based PCR testing, with samples obtained endoscopically, may offer superior sensitivity. Chronic WNV infection can be diagnostically challenging because seroprevalence is high in endemic areas. Paired serologic titers (2-4 weeks apart) using the plaque reduction neutralization test represent the diagnostic gold standard: a fourfold rise in titer indicates recent infection. Radiographic imaging may reveal hepatomegaly and splenomegaly. Hematologic changes may include anemia, heterophilic leukocytosis (sometimes with toxic cell changes), and monocytosis.



Neurologic differential diagnoses include other infectious causes of meningoencephalitis (e.g., Sarcocystis falcatula, Baylisascaris procyonis), epizootic vacuolar myelinopathy in piscivorous species in the southeastern United States, head trauma, and heavy metal toxicosis. The most common postmortem findings in WNV-infected raptors are subacute myocarditis and encephalitis. Supportive care including fluids, nutritional support, and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs may be beneficial; in one retrospective study, up to 60% of affected raptors recovered. Notably, birds presenting with neurologic signs were as likely to recover as those presenting with other clinical signs. Vaccination affords only partial protection and is ineffective in birds younger than 8 weeks of age due to interference from maternal antibodies.
