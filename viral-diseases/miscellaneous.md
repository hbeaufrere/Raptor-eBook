## Herpesvirus

Herpesvirus infection in raptors is most commonly acquired through ingestion of infected prey, particularly columbiform species. Most clinical herpesvirus infections in Accipitriformes, Falconiformes, and Strigiformes are caused by columbid herpesvirus 1. Among owls, barn owls, barred owls, and tawny owls have demonstrated resistance to experimental infection. Unilateral keratoconjunctivitis has been described in Falconiformes and in surviving owls. The fatality rate is high. Pharyngeal ulceration and necrosis have been reported in great horned owls, snowy owls, and Eurasian eagle owls. Characteristic lesions include severe hepatosplenitis and multifocal necrosis of lymphoid organs with typical intranuclear Cowdry type A inclusion bodies. Acyclovir has been proposed as a treatment option, though scientific evidence supporting its efficacy in raptors is limited.



## Poxvirus

Avian poxvirus infection in raptors is principally acquired through arthropod bites or via direct or indirect contact with infected wild birds through skin abrasions. Three clinical forms are recognized: cutaneous, diphtheritic (affecting the oral cavity and upper digestive tract), and septicemic. Raised, circumscribed lesions typically develop on nonfeathered skin areas, including around the beak and eyes and on the feet. In severe cases, oral lesions may prevent the bird from feeding. Definitive diagnosis is obtained through histopathologic examination of biopsied lesions and PCR testing. Eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies (Bollinger bodies) are characteristic histological findings. Management of infected birds includes isolation and supportive care. Prevention involves reducing arthropod (especially mosquito) exposure and disinfecting any materials that have been in contact with infected birds using dilute sodium hypochlorite.



## Adenovirus

Fowl adenovirus has been documented in various raptor species and is transmitted through ingestion of infected prey, as freezing alone does not inactivate the virus. Inclusion body hepatitis caused by fowl adenovirus has been reported as a fatal condition in Falconiformes and Accipitriformes. A distinct adenovirus (falcon adenovirus, with two identified strains) has also been isolated from falconiform species. Peregrine falcons appear to be the primary reservoir for falcon adenovirus, with fecal shedding documented. Aplomado falcons are particularly susceptible to adenoviral infections, and co-housing with peregrine falcons is discouraged. Clinical signs include hemorrhagic diarrhea, anorexia, neurologic signs, and acute death. Pathologic findings include hemorrhagic enteritis, splenomegaly, and hepatitis with intranuclear inclusion bodies. Definitive diagnosis is achieved through conventional PCR, real-time PCR, or in situ hybridization. Currently, only supportive care is available for treatment.
