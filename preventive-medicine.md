# Preventive Medicine

A preventive health program for raptors encompasses an annual physical examination, beak and talon maintenance, fecal parasite screening, and vaccination where indicated by regional disease prevalence. During physical examinations, the plantar surfaces of the feet merit particular scrutiny in sedentary birds and falcons. Beak trimming is generally unnecessary unless the bird has a malocclusion, a beak crack, or deformity related to prior trauma. Talon trimming may be warranted to prevent self-inflicted injuries.



Fecal examination should be performed routinely to detect coccidian oocysts (particularly in Falconiformes and Strigiformes) and ascarid eggs (in all raptors). Two fecal examinations per year—before and after the show or hunting season—are generally recommended. Most gastrointestinal parasites of raptors have direct life cycles, and infestations in confined environments can escalate rapidly, especially in juvenile and debilitated birds.



Commercially available West Nile virus (WNV) vaccines have been evaluated in falcons but not, to date, in other raptor species. Available studies indicate that two commercial formulations provided only partial protection against experimental challenge. A three-dose primary vaccination series proved more protective than a two-dose series. The potential benefits and risks of vaccination with available products must be weighed against the likelihood of infection in a given region.



For falconry birds, sound training and hunting practices are fundamental to maintaining health. The bird's weight should never be reduced to the point of causing poor body condition, and the training process should proceed gradually. Underweight birds are a common problem among inexperienced falconers and frequently become predisposed to a range of diseases as a consequence of immunosuppression.
